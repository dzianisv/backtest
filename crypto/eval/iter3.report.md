Now layer your actual book: COIN's beta means **a −50% BTC move plausibly drags COIN further** (it already fell deeper than BTC off the high). So the real stress test isn't "can I survive BTC −50%" — it's **"can I survive my 30% COIN position AND a new BTC tranche both halving together?"** They're 0.52-correlated and both crypto-beta — in a risk-off flush they move as one. Housel (`05`): *survival is the whole game; avoid ruin at all costs; the goal is to never be a forced seller.*

The honest answer depends on the **unspecified remainder of your book** — which the brief flags as a data gap. If crypto (COIN + any BTC) stays inside the **~$0.5M risk-capital ring-fence** and the $1.5M reserve is genuinely untouchable, a −50% is survivable-by-design and this is a position-sizing decision. If a BTC tranche pushes total crypto past that ring-fence, you're risking what you have and need — and no entry price fixes that.

## 4. Is a tranche scale-in sound? (behaviorally — yes, as discipline)
Housel doesn't tell you *whether* to buy, but on *how*: a pre-committed tranche plan is the **"reasonable, not rational"** move (`05`). It (a) removes the single-day timing bet you can't win the day before FOMC, (b) builds **room for error** so a revisit to $61k or $50k is something you *buy into* rather than panic over, and (c) keeps you from converting a paper drawdown into a forced sale. The discipline of tranching is sound *regardless* of the buy verdict — but tranching is a behavioral wrapper, **not** a license to exceed your crypto ring-fence. A scale-in that ends at a non-survivable total size is just a slower way to over-concentrate.

## 5. One guardrail rule
**Size the COIN + any new BTC as ONE crypto-beta book, and cap that combined book at your pre-set crypto ring-fence (within the ~$0.5M risk capital) — sized so the whole thing halving leaves you a non-forced seller. If a BTC tranche can't fit under that cap, the answer isn't a better entry, it's no tranche.** (Housel: *"More than big returns, I want to be financially unbreakable" — preventing one forced sale beats picking dozens of winners.*)
