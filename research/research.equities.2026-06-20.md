## Behavioral guardrail (non-voting)

**Morgan Housel — Behavioral Guardrail**

*"What AI supply chain stocks will surge next after NVDA?"* is the question of someone playing a different game than they think they're playing. It presupposes a relay race — that AI capex momentum transfers cleanly from one ticker to the next in a predictable sequence. That narrative is seductive precisely because NVDA's run already happened and feels like evidence. It isn't. Tails drive everything: NVDA's specific dominance (CUDA moat, hyperscaler relationships, timing) was a low-probability outcome that looks inevitable in retrospect. The panel is implicitly reasoning from a realized tail event as if it were a template. That is survivorship bias laundered as pattern.

**FOMO-vs-Anchoring:** The panel is doing both simultaneously. NVDA's 10x run is the anchor — every other asset is being evaluated against "could this be the next NVDA?" That framing inflates probability estimates for all of them because the comparison implicitly assumes the same macro regime continues. Meanwhile FOMO pressures the panel toward near-term catalysts (Jun 24 earnings, Investor Day) as triggers — which is short-game behavior dressed up as a thesis.

**Staged Scale-In Soundness:** Staged scale-in is correct instinct, but only works if the tranches are genuinely spaced across time and price levels that would feel psychologically horrific, not just mechanically lower. A panel that calls AVGO "oversold at -15% ATH" and recommends adding is implicitly assuming the floor is near. Room for error must be sized for the range of outcomes that feel unthinkable, not the ones that feel uncomfortable.

**Sizing Survivability:** Consensus across 4+ seats creates hidden sizing risk. When seats all agree on AVGO, the natural human response is to overweight it — consensus feels like safety. It isn't. If AVGO is wrong, all four seats are wrong simultaneously. Behavioral rule: consensus is when you should size most conservatively, not most aggressively.

**Per-asset guardrails:**
- NVDA: Don't use your own recent winner as the baseline for what "normal" returns look like.
- AVGO: Four seats agreeing doesn't make a position safer; it makes position sizing harder to calibrate honestly.
- MU: A Fwd P/E of ~9 is only "cheap" relative to where it was. Cyclical semis can stay cheap or get cheaper for years.
- TSM: "Infrastructure moat" is real; so is the scenario where the moat becomes inaccessible. If you can't hold through a Taiwan-strait headline that drops the stock 40% overnight, don't own it at full size.
- ASML: "Structural moat" is consensus, not variant perception. Ask what the panel knows that isn't already in the price.
- QCOM: Event-driven trades require you to be right about timing, direction, and magnitude. Name the game before sizing.
- AMD: "Overbought but growth is real" is a non-decision dressed as nuance. Don't hold a position you can't articulate a five-year thesis for.
- AMAT/LRCX: Equipment names are leveraged plays on capex timing. By the time evidence of cycle turn appears in fundamentals, stocks will have moved.
- ARM: Pre-commit to conditions under which AVOID becomes a watch. Without it, FOMO will pressure a late, oversized entry.
- INTC: Set an explicit falsification condition before buying. Without it, sunk-cost fallacy keeps the position alive long after thesis is dead.
- SNDK: Per project memory, SNDK was previously flagged as a flagship exclusion. Burden of proof for re-entry is higher than usual. Explicitly re-examine whether the prior flag was resolved or just forgotten.

**Overall process check:** The panel has recency-and-narrative bias baked in. Almost every bullish call is consistent with the supercycle continuing. There is no seat stress-testing the scenario where hyperscaler capex disappoints in H2 2026, or where NVDA's dominance actually suppresses the rest of the supply chain (winner-take-most dynamics). The panel is running confirmation searches, not falsification searches.
