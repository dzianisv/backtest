- **Game B (months):** The medium-term macro player waiting for the July FOMC hike to pass, DXY to reverse, and ETF flows to turn structurally positive. This is a real thesis. It requires sitting through further drawdown with pre-committed tranches.

- **Game C (years):** The Housel-compatible game. Buying Bitcoin at or below the 200-week MA and holding through the noise. Historically, this has not required any understanding of DVOL, put skew, or Howell cycle positioning — just the conviction to hold.

**The brief mixes all three without clearly separating them.** A person who buys T1 today thinking they are playing Game C but emotionally exits when ETH hits $1,500 (Polymarket 80.6% probability) was actually playing a shorter-horizon game they didn't admit to themselves. Housel: "Most forecasting errors aren't about what happens — they're about confusing which game you're playing" (`references/04-compounding-patience-time.md`).

**The single most important behavioral question before any tranche is deployed: what is the holding period, written down, committed to before entry?** If the answer is "it depends on how it goes," the person is in Game A disguised as Game C. That is the most common and most expensive mistake in dip-buying.

## 3. FOMO-VS-ANCHORING TRAP — EXPLICITLY ASSESSED

The brief requests assessment of this trap. Both are live.

**Anchoring is the primary trap here, not FOMO.**

The brief is saturated with anchoring language: "-45.9% from 52w high," "-66% from ATH," "8 consecutive monthly red candles," "-76.9% from SOL ATH." Every one of these figures anchors the reader to a prior peak price. But prior peak prices are not fundamental fair values — they are what someone else paid at peak euphoria. A 45% decline from a speculative top does not make something cheap in absolute terms. It makes it less expensive than it was at the most irrational moment.

The brief partially corrects for this by using on-chain realized prices and MVRV-Z, which are more meaningful anchors. But the anchoring effect is still present: "BTC at -45.9% from its high" triggers the loss-aversion-adjacent sense that it must bounce back to that level. It does not have to. NUPL never went negative this cycle (the brief acknowledges this), meaning full capitulation by prior-bear standards has not been reached. The anchoring trap is reading "deeply oversold" as equivalent to "guaranteed to recover."

**FOMO is a secondary trap, concentrated in HYPE.**

HYPE made an all-time high three days ago. The brief correctly flags this. The behavioral trap is the implicit framing: every other asset in the brief is "beaten down," so HYPE's inclusion as a "high-conviction alt" creates a comparison in which HYPE looks like the winner — which triggers the FOMO pattern of "I need to own the thing that is working." The brief correctly categorizes HYPE as "not beaten down," but places it in the same priority list anyway. A Housel-trained reader notes: buying something at 13% off its 3-day-old ATH because it has strong fundamentals is a different behavior from buying something 66% off its prior high. Mixing both under "dip-buy thesis" conflates two separate decisions.

**The FOMO-vs-anchoring assessment:** Anchoring to prior highs is the larger behavioral risk across BTC/ETH/SOL. FOMO is the risk on HYPE. A staged scale-in directly mitigates anchoring (it does not require calling the exact bottom) and partially mitigates FOMO (it forces a slower pace than "buy now").

## 4. IS A STAGED SCALE-IN SOUND?

**Yes — and it is the Housel-compatible answer, for specific behavioral reasons that the brief does not fully articulate.**

The brief recommends staged tranches (30/35/35 for BTC, 30/40/30 for ETH, 20/40/40 for SOL). This is correct mechanics. But Housel's framing of *why* is more important than the mechanics:

**A staged scale-in is not primarily a return optimization — it is a survival and commitment mechanism.**

The brief frames tranches as: "T2 at $54K because that's near the realized price." That is a valuation rationale. Housel would add the behavioral rationale: the reason you deploy in stages is that if ETH touches $1,500 after you deployed T1 at $1,680, you will not panic-sell T1 (which would convert a paper loss into a permanent one) *because you already planned to buy more at $1,500.* The staged entry is what psychologically allows you to hold through the drawdown, not merely what optimizes your average cost. **The plan is the product, not the price targets** (`references/05-getting-vs-keeping-wealth.md`).

The brief's invalidation levels matter for this same reason: they should be set *before* entry, written down, and committed to. If the invalidation level feels uncomfortable to commit to before entry — if the honest answer is "I'll know when to get out when I see it" — the staged entry will not perform its behavioral function. The person will exit at the first violent move down, regardless of which tranche they are in.

**One important gap:** the brief does not address what happens to the investor's non-crypto capital when they deploy into crypto tranches. Housel's room-for-error principle asks: if all tranches deploy and BTC falls to $45K and ETH falls to $1,100, does the investor still have enough cash/stable assets outside crypto that they are not *forced* to sell? The brief correctly says "no portfolio provided" — but this is the single most important question. Forced selling at the worst moment is how compounding is permanently broken. **Position sizes are only survivable if non-crypto reserves remain intact regardless of scenario** (`references/06-wealth-saving-and-freedom.md`).

## 5. IS SIZING SURVIVABLE TO -50%?

**This is the right question. The brief's tranche structure is survivable only if total crypto-earmarked capital represents a known, capped fraction of total net worth.**

The brief expresses sizing as percentages of "crypto-earmarked allocation." That is an appropriate market-level framing for a no-portfolio question. But Housel's survivability test applies at the total-net-worth level, not the allocation-bucket level.

The test: if BTC falls to $31K (approximately -50% from today), ETH falls to $840, and SOL falls to $34 — all within the range of prior bear cycles — does the total portfolio loss remain within the range the investor previously committed to surviving without changing behavior?

The brief's priority order (BTC 45-50% of crypto allocation, ETH 25-30%, HYPE 10-15%, SOL 10-15%) is internally reasonable. The sizing is survivable *within the crypto bucket*. Whether the crypto bucket itself is sized survivably depends on total portfolio context that is not available — and the brief correctly does not invent it.

**The Housel guardrail here:** before executing any tranche, the investor should be able to state aloud: "If every position in this plan falls 50% from my average entry price, the total dollar loss I would experience is $_____, and I can absorb that loss without changing my lifestyle, without being forced to sell, and without losing sleep in a way that would cause me to exit the plan." If that sentence cannot be completed with a real number and honest agreement, the allocation is too large, regardless of how sound the thesis is (`references/05-getting-vs-keeping-wealth.md`).

## 6. ONE GUARDRAIL RULE

**The brief contains extensive analysis of what might happen. It contains almost no analysis of what the investor would do if the opposite of the thesis occurs — and how long they would hold before concluding they are wrong.**

Housel's single most important behavioral rule for a situation like this:

**"Define the exit before the entry, in writing, and refuse to move the invalidation level downward after entry."**

The brief gives invalidation levels (BTC weekly close below $48K with specific confirmations; ETH weekly close below $1,500 with specific conditions; SOL weekly close below $45 with REV decline). These are reasonable. The behavioral trap that destroys their value: after BTC closes below $48K and the investor has unrealized losses, the investor will search for reasons why this particular close is "different" — the Fed is about to pivot, the whale accumulation data just came in, DXY is showing signs of reversing. This is the moving-goalpost pattern applied to risk management. The MVRV-Z is even lower now, so it must be even cheaper — and then the NUPL is approaching zero, so now the real bottom is near. Each step down produces a new rationalization for not respecting the invalidation.

**The guardrail rule: the invalidation level is not a prediction about price; it is a commitment about your own behavior.** It says: "I am willing to be wrong about this trade, and this is the level at which I will admit I was wrong." Moving it downward after entry is not updating on new information — it is refusing to admit error. That is the behavior that turns a thesis-driven drawdown into a permanent capital loss.

The brief's desk-level analysis is rigorous. The behavioral discipline to execute the invalidation when triggered is the weakest link — and that is not a data problem.
