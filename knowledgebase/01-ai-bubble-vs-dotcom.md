# 01 — Are We in an AI Bubble Like the Dot-Com Bubble?

*Research note, May 2026. Educational analysis, not advice. Sources at the end.*

## Bottom line

US equities in 2026 are at or beyond dot-com-peak valuation extremes on the most-cited
gauges, and **more concentrated** than in 2000. The decisive difference is **earnings**:
the AI leaders (NVIDIA above all) generate enormous real profits, unlike many 2000-era
pre-profit dot-coms. So the market is best described as **expensive and dangerously
concentrated, with a partly-justified valuation resting on capex assumptions that may not
pay off** — not a carbon copy of 2000, but sharing enough of its DNA that the 2000-2002
drawdown is a legitimate base-rate scenario to plan around.

## 1. Valuation: now vs the dot-com peak

| Metric | May 2026 | Dot-com peak (1999-2000) | Long-run norm |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Shiller CAPE** | ~41.6 | 44.19 (Dec 1999) | ~17 mean |
| **Buffett Indicator** (mkt cap / GDP) | ~219-235% (record 230% Jan 2026) | ~137-146% | ~100-120% |
| **Top-10 forward P/E** | ~31 | ~43 | — |
| **Goldman 10-yr S&P forecast** | ~6.5%/yr nominal | — | ~10% historical |

- CAPE ~41.6 is the **2nd-highest reading in 144 years** — only Dec 1999 (44.19) was higher.
  1929 peaked ~32; 2007 peaked ~27.
- The Buffett Indicator is **worse than 2000** — by this measure today is *more* extreme.
- But the top-10 *valuation premium* over the rest of the index (~50%) is **half** of 2000's
  (>100%). NVIDIA's forward P/E (mid-20s to mid-40s depending on date/estimate) is a fraction
  of **Cisco's 2000 peak of P/E >200, EV/sales ~31**.

## 2. Concentration — the strongest bubble parallel (arguably worse than 2000)

- The **"Magnificent Seven"** (NVDA, MSFT, AAPL, GOOGL/GOOG, AMZN, META, +7th) were
  **~34-35%** of the S&P 500 in late 2025, up from 12.3% in 2015.
- The **top 10 stocks** are **~38-41%** of the index — ten companies outweigh the other 490.
  At the 2000 peak the top 10 were only **~27%**.
- Year-end 2025 weights: NVDA ~7.3%, AAPL ~6.5%, MSFT ~5.8%, GOOGL+GOOG ~6.1%, AMZN ~4.0%,
  META ~2.7%, AVGO ~2.6%, TSLA ~2.4%.

**This is the single most bubble-like feature — and it's worse than 2000.** Buying a
cap-weight S&P 500 fund today is a ~40% bet on ten AI-correlated mega-caps.

## 3. Bull case vs bear case

**Bull (largely fact-based):**
- Real earnings. NVIDIA FY2025: **revenue $130.5B (+114% YoY)**, GAAP EPS +147%, gross
  margin ~73-75%. The opposite of Pets.com / Webvan.
- The Mag-7 fund most capex from operating cash flow.
- Top-10 valuation premium is half of 2000's; breadth is broadening in 2026 (equal-weight RSP
  +5.5% YTD vs cap-weight SPY −0.2% in the snapshot; ~56% of stocks above 200-dma — middling).

**Bear (mix of fact and opinion):**
- **Circular financing.** A recursive web links MSFT/NVDA/AMZN/META/GOOG ↔ OpenAI/Anthropic as
  simultaneous supplier, customer, and investor. NVIDIA agreed to invest up to **$100B in OpenAI**
  while OpenAI commits to ~10GW of NVIDIA infrastructure; OpenAI agreed to ~$250B of Microsoft cloud.
- **Capex vs revenue gap.** ~$50-60B/yr AI revenue against **$500B+/yr capex** (~$8-10 spend per
  $1 revenue); hyperscaler debt issuance hit **$121B in 2025** (~4× normal).
- **Michael Burry** (Nov 2025) bought >$1B notional puts on NVDA/PLTR, calling NVIDIA "a Cisco at
  the center of it all," arguing GPU depreciation schedules flatter earnings (Enron comparison).
- **MIT NANDA study (2025):** **95% of enterprise GenAI pilots delivered no measurable P&L return.**

**The unresolved swing factor:** whether ~$500B/yr of AI capex earns a return. Credible bulls and
bears both exist; this is genuinely uncertain.

## 4. The base rate that should scare you (2000-2002)

- **Nasdaq Composite** peaked 5,048.62 (Mar 10, 2000), fell **~78%** to 1,114.11 (Oct 9, 2002).
- It did not recover the 2000 high until **April 2015 — ~15 years.**
- The **S&P 500 fell ~49%** peak-to-trough and took ~7 years to recover nominally — then
  re-crashed in 2008.

Our own backtest (note 03) reproduces this: **2000-2009 was a "lost decade"** — S&P −0.9%/yr,
QQQ −6.8%/yr — even though the leaders were "real" companies (Cisco, Intel, Microsoft survived;
their *stocks* still fell 60-80%). Being a great company does not protect the *stock price* from
a great valuation.

## 5. What the institutions forecast

- **GMO:** ~**−6.3% real**/yr for US large caps over 7 years (most bearish).
- **Vanguard (VCMM):** **3.5-5.5%** nominal/yr US equities over 10 years.
- **Goldman Sachs:** ~**6.5%**/yr nominal S&P over 10 years — "well below" the ~10% historical avg.

These low forecasts are a *function of* today's high starting valuations (the CAPE → forward-return
relationship). High price paid = low future return, mechanically.

## So — why not just S&P 500 or QQQ?

You can still own them, but understand what you're buying at these levels:
1. **Concentration risk:** ~40% of the S&P is 10 AI-correlated names; QQQ is even more concentrated.
2. **Valuation risk:** entry CAPE ~41 historically maps to low/negative 10-yr real returns.
3. **Drawdown/recovery risk:** the base rate for bursting concentration bubbles is −50% to −78%
   and 7-15 years to recover. Sequence-of-returns matters enormously if you might need the money.

The answer is **not** "predict the crash and sit in cash" (forecasting tops is a loser's game —
see notes 02/03). It's **diversify the core away from a single regime and add crisis-alpha overlays**
so you participate in upside but survive a −50% AI unwind. That's what notes 02-08 build.
