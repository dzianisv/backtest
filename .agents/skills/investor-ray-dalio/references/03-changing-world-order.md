# The Changing World Order & the Big Cycle of Empires — Ray Dalio's Framework
> Source: Dalio, *Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order: Why Nations Succeed and Fail* (Avid Reader Press, 2021); the LinkedIn/economicprinciples.org essay series "The Changing World Order" (linkedin.com/pulse/changing-world-order-ray-dalio-1f) and animated video; "Country Power Index" charts (economicprinciples.org/downloads/cwo-power-index.pdf); the Sept-2020 study PDF (asiabusinesscouncil.org/.../The-Changing-World-Order_Ray-Dalio.pdf). Current corroboration: Fortune, 14 Mar 2026. Distilled 2026-06-07.

## Core thesis
The same forces that drive the rise and decline of empires recur across the last ~500 years (Dutch → British → American, with China rising). Each leading empire follows an archetypal **Big Cycle of ~250 years (± ~150)** with three phases — Rise, Top, Decline — measured by ~18 determinants that cluster into **eight types of power**. The Big Cycle is propelled by the interaction of three sub-cycles: the **long-term debt/money cycle**, the **internal order/disorder cycle** (driven by wealth, values and political gaps), and the **external order/disorder cycle** (great-power rivalry). Reserve-currency status is both the ultimate prize and the seed of decline: it lets the leading power over-borrow, which eventually debases the currency and erodes the empire. Dalio judges the US to be in late-stage decline and China in ascent, with the post-1945 order breaking down — "more analogous to pre-1945 than post-1945."

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Big Cycle of empires**: Rise → Top → Decline, ~250 yrs avg. Peaceful/creative periods outlast disorderly/war periods ~**5:1**.
- **The eight types of power** (measured to build a country Power Index): (1) education, (2) competitiveness, (3) innovation & technology, (4) economic output, (5) share of world trade, (6) military strength, (7) financial-center / capital-markets strength, (8) reserve-currency status. (He also tracks debt burden, internal order, and a few others among ~18 determinants.)
- **The self-reinforcing rise**: strong education → competitiveness & innovation → output & trade share → military to protect trade → financial center → reserve currency.
- **The decline mechanics**: reserve-currency status → over-borrowing & money printing → debt & currency debasement → loss of competitiveness → internal wealth/values gaps & conflict → external challenge → loss of reserve status. The currency stays reserve *after* the empire peaks because habit outlasts the strengths that earned it.
- **Three big interacting forces today** (unprecedented in Dalio's lifetime): (1) huge debts + zero/negative rates + money printing; (2) the largest internal **wealth, values, and political gaps** in ~a century → populism left and right; (3) the rise of a **true rival power (China)** challenging the incumbent (US).
- **Internal-order stages** (1→6): from strong/peaceful through worsening finances and conflict to civil-war/revolution; Dalio places the US around **Stage 5** (the dangerous brink before breakdown).

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- Studied the last **~500 years** and three reserve currencies: Dutch guilder (17th c.), British pound (19th–20th c.), US dollar (20th–21st c.), plus Chinese dynasties from ~600 CE.
- US "rose faster than any prior power," peaked around WWII, now in slow relative decline; China rising fast and is the first rival competitive across "production, trade, technology, geopolitics, and capital markets" (unlike the militarily-only USSR).
- "At no point in our lifetimes have interest rates been so low or negative on so much debt."
- "Wealth, values, and political gaps are now larger than at any other point during my lifetime" (largest since ~1930s).
- (2026) Dalio: "We are now in **Stage 5**," entering "the most dangerous phase of the Big Cycle"; cites "large debts, deficits, and debasements of fiat currencies led by the dollar and the rise in the gold price," "pre-civil-war-type developments," and "the breaking down of the post-1945 multilateral, rules-based international order."
- Risk window: war risk is highest when two powers approach **comparable** strength — exactly the current US–China configuration.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Place any country on the Rise→Top→Decline arc using the 8 powers; favor rising powers' assets/currencies, discount declining powers' long-dated claims.
2. Treat **reserve-currency over-borrowing** as the master signal of late-stage decline: rising debt + monetization + gold appreciation vs. the currency = reduce that currency's bonds, add gold/real assets and rising-power exposure.
3. Monitor **internal-disorder gauges** (wealth/values/political gaps, populism, civil-conflict markers) — escalation argues for diversification across jurisdictions and lower risk-taking.
4. Monitor **external-disorder gauges** (US–China trade/tech/capital/military friction); when powers near parity, raise the probability weight on conflict and "capital war" (capital controls, sanctions, asset seizure) — diversify across countries and custodians.
5. Map portfolio jurisdiction risk: don't concentrate in the assets, currency, and legal system of a single late-cycle incumbent.
6. Use the 5:1 peace:disorder ratio as a base rate — disorder is rarer but far more destructive; hedge the tail, don't bet the cycle ends tomorrow.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- Dalio repeatedly says "nothing is predestined" and that good leadership can change outcomes; the cycle is a tendency, not a prophecy.
- Critics charge the framework is **overfit** to a handful of empires and that ~250-year cycles with ±150-year error bars are too elastic to be predictive.
- His US-decline / China-ascent call is contested — others note US structural strengths (demographics vs. China, energy, capital markets, AI lead) and China's own debt/demographic problems.
- Timing of "decline" is vague; the dollar's reserve dominance has persisted far longer than many cycle-based bears expected.
