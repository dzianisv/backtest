# Economic Moats — Warren Buffett's Framework
> Source: Berkshire letters 1977 (inflation essay), 1983 (Buffalo News/goodwill appendix), 1991 (economic franchise), 1992, 2007 (great/good/gruesome), 2011, 2024; Fortune "How Inflation Swindles the Equity Investor" (1977); annual-meeting Q&A on pricing power. Distilled 2026-06-07. All reachable.

## Core thesis
A business worth owning is one protected by a durable competitive advantage — a "moat" — that lets it earn high returns on capital for a long time and defends those returns from competitors who are always trying to take them. The single best evidence of a moat is **pricing power**: the ability to raise prices without losing customers and without a quality investment. Buffett distinguishes an "economic franchise" (a product that is needed/desired, has no close substitute, and is not price-regulated — so it can be mispriced and badly managed and still survive) from a mere "business" (which survives only by being a low-cost operator or by short supply). He sorts the world into great, good, and gruesome businesses by one ratio: return on incremental invested capital. The great business compounds owner capital internally at high rates; the gruesome one consumes capital to grow and earns nothing. Inflation is the acid test: moat businesses with low capital intensity and pricing power pass inflation through; capital-heavy "bad" businesses are swindled by it.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **The moat**: "a wide and long-lasting moat around an economic castle" defended by an "honest lord." Sources: brand/intangibles, pricing power, low-cost production, switching costs, network effects, and (rarely durable) scale.
- **Economic franchise vs. business** (1991 letter): franchise = needed/wanted product, no close substitute, unregulated price → can raise prices and tolerate mediocre management. Business = competes on cost or scarcity.
- **Great / Good / Gruesome** (2007 letter): Great = high return on capital that *rises* and needs little incremental capital (See's). Good = decent returns but needs capital to grow (FlightSafety). Gruesome = grows fast, eats capital, earns nothing (airlines).
- **Return on invested capital, not growth, is the prize.** Growth only creates value when funded at returns above the cost of capital.
- **Pricing power as the moat test**: "The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power."
- **The equity coupon** (1977): ROE behaves like a bond coupon stuck near ~12%; inflation doesn't lift it, so unleveraged capital-heavy equity is a poor inflation hedge.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **See's Candies**: bought 1972 for $25M on ~$4M pre-tax earnings and ~$8M tangible net assets. By later letters it had earned **well over $2 billion pre-tax cumulatively on a tiny incremental capital base** — the archetype of a great business: brand + box-of-chocolates emotional pricing power let it raise prices yearly with almost no added capital. "The business required little capital… and could raise prices regularly."
- **Coca-Cola**: brand and global distribution = franchise; "share of mind" → pricing power and reinvestment-light growth.
- **Airlines** (gruesome): "a durable competitive advantage has proven elusive ever since the days of the Wright Brothers… if a farsighted capitalist had been present at Kitty Hawk, he would have done his successors a huge favor by shooting Orville down." Massive capital, commodity product, no pricing power.
- **Inflation mechanism (1977)**: corporate ROE was stuck ~12% across 1946–1975 regardless of inflation; the "coupon" doesn't reset upward, so real returns get swindled. Best inflation defenses: businesses needing little capital that raise prices easily (See's), and great brands — not asset-heavy commodity producers.
- **Goodwill / intangibles (1983 appendix)**: economic goodwill (the moat) tends to *grow* in value with inflation while accounting goodwill amortizes — a low-tangible-asset franchise is worth more than its book suggests.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Test pricing power first: can this business raise prices ~with inflation-plus and keep its customers? If not, it has no moat — pass or treat as cyclical/commodity.
2. Compute return on invested capital and, more importantly, return on *incremental* capital. Classify Great / Good / Gruesome. Concentrate in Great; own Good only at a discount; avoid Gruesome regardless of growth.
3. Identify the moat *source* explicitly (brand, low-cost, switching costs, network) and ask what would breach it. A moat you can't name is a moat you can't trust.
4. Prefer low-capital-intensity franchises: high returns + low reinvestment needs = a cash machine that funds itself and resists inflation.
5. In an inflationary regime, overweight pricing-power, asset-light franchises; underweight capital-hungry commodity producers (the "equity coupon" trap).
6. Reward durability over slope: a slower grower with a widening moat beats a fast grower whose advantage is eroding.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- **Moats erode** — Dexter Shoe's low-cost moat was destroyed by foreign labor; newspapers (Buffalo News) had a franchise that the internet dismantled. He now stresses moats must be *re-tested*, not assumed permanent.
- The framework can **undervalue platform/network businesses early** (Amazon, Google) because their moats looked like uneconomic growth before scale — a blind spot in the 2010s growth regime.
- "Pricing power above all" can miss volume-driven low-cost compounders that win without raising prices (Costco's reverse-pricing-power moat is the exception he and Munger admired).
- Capital-light is a luxury; in capital-intensive regulated businesses (BNSF, energy utilities) he accepts lower-but-stable returns for size and bond-like predictability — a deliberate departure from the See's ideal as Berkshire grew too big to live on See's-sized deals.

## Memorable quotes
- "The single most important decision in evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business."
- "The most important thing for me is figuring out how big a moat there is around the business… I want a business with a moat around it, with a very valuable castle in the middle."
- "Now let's move to the gruesome… The worst sort of business is one that grows rapidly, requires significant capital to engender the growth, and then earns little or no money. Think airlines."
- "A truly great business must have an enduring 'moat' that protects excellent returns on invested capital."
- "Leaving the question of price aside, the best business to own is one that over an extended period can employ large amounts of incremental capital at very high rates of return."
