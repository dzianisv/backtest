# Intrinsic Value & Margin of Safety — Warren Buffett's Framework
> Source: Berkshire letters 1986 (owner earnings/Scott Fetzer), 1992 (intrinsic value & DCF), 1994 (macro-ignoring), Owner's Manual (intrinsic value definition, Mr. Market via 1987 letter & ch.8 of *The Intelligent Investor*); Graham *The Intelligent Investor* ch. 8 & 20; Fortune 1999. Distilled 2026-06-07. All reachable.

## Core thesis
Intrinsic value is "the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life" — nothing more, nothing less. It is not book value and not market price; it is an estimate, a range, that two honest analysts will compute differently because it depends on future cash flows and a discount rate. The right earnings number to discount is **owner earnings**, not reported EPS, because GAAP ignores the capital a business must reinvest just to stand still. You buy only when price sits well below your conservative estimate of value — the **margin of safety**, Graham's "three most important words in investing." The market (Graham's manic-depressive **Mr. Market**) exists to serve you, not instruct you: it offers a price every day, and you are free to ignore it until it offers one absurdly cheap or rich. Crucially, value is computed bottom-up from the business; Buffett refuses to base it on macro forecasts (interest-rate calls, recession timing) because they are unknowable and irrelevant to a great business held long enough.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Intrinsic value = DCF of all future cash to owners.** A range, not a point. "The only logical way to evaluate the relative attractiveness of investments."
- **Owner earnings** (1986): reported earnings + depreciation/amortization/non-cash charges − the average maintenance capex required to preserve competitive position and unit volume. This is the true cash, vs. reported EPS which flatters capital-heavy firms.
- **Margin of safety** (Graham): buy at a large discount to value so errors and bad luck don't cost you. "The function of the margin of safety is, in essence, that of rendering unnecessary an accurate estimate of the future."
- **Mr. Market** (Graham, ch. 8): a moody partner quoting buy/sell prices daily; useful only when his price is foolish. "Mr. Market is there to serve you, not to guide you."
- **Price is what you pay; value is what you get.**
- **The two-column worth**: value an investment holding (cash/securities) separately from the operating businesses' earning power — Berkshire's own "two columns" of look-through value.
- **Ignore the macro**: forecast the business, not the economy. "We will continue to ignore political and economic forecasts."

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Owner earnings example (Scott Fetzer, 1986 letter)**: "Company O" and "Company N" are the same business with different accounting; the GAAP purchase-accounting depreciation overstates real capital consumption, so reported earnings understate owner earnings. The lesson: discounted-cash-flow valuation must use maintenance-capex-adjusted cash, not EPS.
- **Two-column / look-through earnings**: Buffett valued Berkshire by (1) per-share investments held and (2) pre-tax earnings of the operating businesses (ex-investment income), arguing the market should value the second column at a business multiple — a practical intrinsic-value scaffold.
- **Discount rate**: he uses the long-term Treasury yield as the base discount rate (not a CAPM beta), demanding a margin of safety rather than padding the rate; "interest rates act on financial valuations the way gravity acts on matter" (1999 Fortune).
- **The 1999 valuation call**: with corporate-profits/GDP elevated and rates low, he warned equity returns over the next 17 years would be far below the 1982–1999 boom — value computed from earnings, not extrapolated price.
- **Mr. Market in action**: 1987 crash, 1973–74, 2008–09 — he treated each plunge as Mr. Market offering value below intrinsic worth.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Estimate intrinsic value as a *range* via DCF of **owner earnings** (EPS + non-cash charges − true maintenance capex), discounted at the long-bond yield. Never use reported EPS uncritically.
2. Require a margin of safety: buy only meaningfully below the low end of your value range (the bigger the uncertainty, the bigger the required discount). If no margin, do nothing.
3. Treat the quoted price as Mr. Market's offer, not a verdict. Act only when his price is absurd relative to your independent value; otherwise ignore the ticker.
4. Build value bottom-up from the business; explicitly refuse to anchor it on macro forecasts (rate calls, recession timing, GDP).
5. Watch interest rates as the "gravity" on all valuations — low rates justify higher multiples but don't suspend the law; demand margin of safety regardless.
6. Cross-check with the two-column method: value cash/securities at face, operating earnings at a sober business multiple; sum and compare to price.
7. Prefer being approximately right to precisely wrong: a conservative range plus a discount beats a false-precision point estimate.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- Intrinsic value is **subjective and forward-looking** — "calculating intrinsic value is not so simple… two people looking at the same set of facts will almost inevitably come up with at least slightly different figures." Garbage-in on cash-flow assumptions yields garbage-out.
- **Owner earnings require judgment** on "maintenance" vs. "growth" capex, which firms don't report — the number is an estimate, not a GAAP line.
- The deep-value / large-discount discipline **lagged in the 2010s** when high-multiple compounders kept compounding; strict Graham margin-of-safety would have kept you out of the decade's best stocks. Buffett himself migrated from "cigar butts" to "wonderful business at a fair price" (Munger's influence) — the margin can come from quality/durability, not only price.
- Ignoring the macro works for multi-decade holders of great businesses; it is riskier for leveraged or short-horizon players for whom rate shocks dominate.
