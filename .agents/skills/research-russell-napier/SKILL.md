# Analytics: The Russell Napier Lens

Apply Russell Napier's framework to a question. This skill is the **synthesis + router**; the detail
lives in `references/`. He is the panel's **financial-repression / "who controls money" seat** — the
political *mechanism* under fiscal dominance, and the inflation camp's structural case. Load the
relevant reference before a load-bearing claim.

## The unifying worldview (everything connects to this)

Napier reasons from **who controls money creation**. Broad money is created by commercial-bank lending,
so whoever steers bank credit controls the money supply — and since COVID, that lever passed from
central banks to **governments**, which now expand and direct credit off-budget via **loan guarantees**
and regulatory diktat ("national capitalism"). This makes central banks "irrelevant" and gives the state
a frictionless printing press. The chosen exit from a debt-soaked system is **financial repression**:
hold interest rates below inflation so **negative real rates** quietly erode the debt over ~15-20 years
(the WWII-to-1979 playbook), while regulation forces **captive savers** (pensions, banks, insurers) to
fund the government. The result is a structural **4-6% inflation regime** — the 40-year disinflation is
over — turbo-charged by the largest investment cycle since the mid-1990s (reshoring, defense, energy
grid, decarbonization). Separately, his **"Anatomy of the Bear"** work dates generational equity bottoms
(deep-value Q/CAPE + the death of a deflation scare). The portfolio answer: don't be the captive
creditor — own gold, equities, real assets; avoid long sovereign bonds.

## How to apply the lens (decision procedure)

1. **Ask who controls money creation right now.** Central bank or government? Are credit guarantees /
   directed lending expanding broad money? That sets the regime.
2. **Test for financial repression.** Are real rates negative and likely to stay so by policy? If yes,
   price assets for a slow, deliberate debasement of the saver — not a bond-friendly disinflation.
3. **Check the capex-boom drivers** (reshoring/defense/energy) — structural cost-push that keeps
   inflation above target.
4. **For equities at extremes, run the Anatomy-of-the-Bear screen** (Q ratio, CAPE, and whether a
   deflation scare has died) before calling a generational bottom.
5. **Give the don't-be-the-captive-creditor allocation:** gold + equities (value, small/mid, non-US,
   capex sectors) over long-duration sovereign bonds; watch for capital-controls risk.
6. **Separate framework from dated view.** His mechanics are evergreen; his positioning decays — for
   "what does he think now", read `05-current-views.md` and re-check the latest "Solid Ground" /
   interview. Note he also flags a *near-term deflation risk* nested inside the long inflation regime.

## Honesty rules (non-negotiable)

- **It's a lens, not gospel.** Present it as "Napier's framework says…". Note where mainstream (and the
  Hunt dissent seat) disagree — Hunt argues the same debt load is deflationary.
- **He has been early / flipped.** A noted deflationist for ~25 years before turning structural-inflation
  in 2020-21; his 2005 deflationary S&P-bottom call never arrived. Carry the caveats.
- **Current positioning decays.** Date every current-view claim; re-check the latest "Solid Ground" /
  interview (and note the nested near-term deflation risk he himself flags).
- **Ground load-bearing claims** in a specific reference/source (via `references/article-index.md`).
- This skill **complements** `investor-lyn-alden` (shares the repression/inflation endgame) and is the
  structural counter to `research-lacy-hunt`; it feeds `regime-detection` / `portfolio-construction`.
  It does not replace the `fundamental-analysis` gate.
