# Behavior Over Intelligence — "No One's Crazy"
> Source: Housel, *The Psychology of Money* (2020, Harriman House), Introduction ("The Greatest Show On Earth") + ch. 1 ("No One's Crazy"). Distilled 2026-06.

## Core thesis
**Doing well with money has little to do with how smart you are and a lot to do with how you behave.** Financial success is not a hard science you solve with a formula; it is a *soft skill* where temperament beats IQ. An ordinary person with a handful of behavioral skills can out-finance a genius who loses control of their emotions — a result that is "unique to finance" (a janitor will never out-operate a heart surgeon, but a janitor can out-invest a Harvard MBA). And because everyone's relationship with money was forged by the tiny, specific slice of history *they* lived through, **no one is crazy**: every money decision makes sense to the person making it in the moment, even when it looks insane to you.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Money is a soft skill, not a math test.** Finance is taught like physics (rules, formulas) but behaves like psychology (emotion, nuance). "Knowing what to do tells you nothing about what happens in your head when you try to do it."
- **No one's crazy.** People raised in different generations, economies, and job markets learn "very different lessons"; their behavior is internally consistent given their experience.
- **Experience > second-hand knowledge.** "What you've experienced is more compelling than what you learn second-hand." Spreadsheets can model the frequency of crashes but not the *feeling* of one.
- **The experience-anchoring bias.** Your personal experience is "maybe 0.00000001% of what's happened in the world, but maybe 80% of how you think the world works."
- **We're all newbies.** Modern saving/investing concepts are practically infants, so bad behavior is expected, not aberrant.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Ronald Read vs Richard Fuscone.** Read, a Vermont janitor/gas-station attendant, saved a little, bought blue chips, *waited for decades*, and died in 2014 with >$8 million (leaving $6m to a hospital and library) — one of <4,000 of the 2.8 million Americans who died that year worth over $8m. Fuscone, a Harvard MBA Merrill Lynch executive, borrowed heavily, and the 2008 crisis left him bankrupt, his Greenwich mansion foreclosed. "Ronald Read was patient; Richard Fuscone was greedy. That's all it took to eclipse the massive education and experience gap."
- **The gold-coin tech executive.** A Wi-Fi-patent genius threw $1,000 gold coins into the Pacific for fun and went broke — "A genius who loses control of their emotions can be a financial disaster."
- **Malmendier & Nagel (NBER, 2006):** 50 years of the Survey of Consumer Finances show "individual investors' willingness to bear risk depends on personal history" — grow up with high inflation, own fewer bonds later; grow up in a strong market, own more stocks. "Not intelligence… just the dumb luck of when and where you were born."
- **Born 1970 vs 1950:** the S&P rose ~10x (real) during the teens/20s of the 1970 cohort and went *nowhere* for the 1950 cohort — two opposite worldviews by birth year.
- **Lottery tickets:** the lowest-income U.S. households spend ~$412/yr on lotto (4x the top group), yet 40% of Americans can't cover a $400 emergency — crazy to you, but "we are paying for a dream."
- **How new this all is:** the 401(k) didn't exist until 1978, the Roth IRA until 1998; index funds are <50 years old. "We're all just newbies."

## How to APPLY (decision rules)
1. **Diagnose behavior before math.** When a decision looks dumb (yours or someone else's), first ask *what experience makes this feel sensible to them?* Judge the person less and the process more.
2. **Don't confuse IQ with discipline.** A credential or high intelligence is not evidence of good money behavior; patience, humility, and emotional control are the actual edge.
3. **Distrust your own certainty.** Your strong views are anchored to your narrow history. Seek out people whose lived experience differs before concluding the market "must" do X.
4. **Respect that emotion will override the spreadsheet.** Build plans you can *feel* your way through, because under fear/greed you will not act like the model.
5. **Forgive the newbie problem.** Bad money habits are partly because the modern system is decades, not millennia, old — expect mistakes and design for them rather than assuming rationality.

## Caveats
- "No one's crazy" is an *explanation*, not an *excuse* — understanding why someone bought lotto tickets or over-levered doesn't make it a good decision; Housel says so explicitly ("still a bad idea").
- The frame can slide into relativism ("everyone's right in their own way"); pair it with the survival/room-for-error discipline so empathy doesn't become a license for ruinous choices.
- Behavior beats intelligence *on average over a lifetime*; it does not mean knowledge is worthless — Housel still wants you to understand compounding, fees, and odds.
