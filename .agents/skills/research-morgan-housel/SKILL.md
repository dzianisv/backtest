# Analytics: The Morgan Housel Lens

Apply Morgan Housel's framework to a money decision or behavior. This skill is the **synthesis +
router**; the detail lives in `references/`. It is the panel's **behavioral guardrail** — not a view
on *what* to buy, but on *how you behave* around whatever you own. It governs the human in the loop:
the saver who can't stop comparing, and the trader who panic-sells the bottom or chases the top — "the
weakest link." Load the relevant reference before a load-bearing claim.

## The unifying worldview (everything connects to this)

Housel's premise: **doing well with money has little to do with how smart you are and a lot to do with
how you behave** — it is a *soft skill*, not a math problem (Ronald Read the janitor died with $8m;
Richard Fuscone the Harvard MBA went bankrupt). Because **no one's crazy** — every person's choices
make sense given the tiny, idiosyncratic slice of history they lived through — judgment should be humble.
**Luck and risk are siblings**, and **tails drive everything**: a few decisions, a few days, a few
holdings produce most of the outcome, so you can be wrong half the time and still do fine. The two
scarcest skills are **"enough"** (getting the goalpost to stop moving) and **patience** — because
compounding's real engine is **time**, not a high return. Getting wealthy takes optimism and risk;
**staying** wealthy takes frugality, paranoia, and above all **survival** — so you **worship room for
error** and avoid ruin. **Wealth is what you don't see** (the unspent option), your **savings rate**
matters more than income or returns, and **controlling your time is the highest dividend money pays.**
Finally, aim to be **reasonable, not coldly rational** — the strategy you can stick with beats the
optimal one you'll abandon.

## Core mental models (the load-bearing ones)

1. **Behavior over intelligence; no one's crazy.** Money is a soft skill; your experiences (≈0.00000001%
   of history) drive ≈80% of your worldview. → `references/01-behavior-over-intelligence.md`
2. **Luck & risk are siblings.** Same force, opposite signs (Gates's Lakeside computer vs Kent Evans).
   Judge the process, not the individual outcome. → `references/02-luck-risk-and-tails.md`
3. **Tails drive everything.** A tiny minority of events/holdings/days produce most results; being wrong
   often is normal. → `references/02-luck-risk-and-tails.md`
4. **Enough / get the goalpost to stop moving.** The hardest skill; happiness = results − expectations;
   don't risk what you have and need for what you don't. → `references/03-enough-and-greed.md`
5. **Compounding = time, not just return.** $81.5bn of Buffett's $84.5bn came after his mid-60s; "Shut
   Up And Wait." → `references/04-compounding-patience-time.md`
6. **Getting vs keeping wealth.** Get = optimism/risk; keep = frugality + paranoia + survival; "I want to
   be financially unbreakable." → `references/05-getting-vs-keeping-wealth.md`
7. **Room for error / margin of safety; avoid ruin.** A barbell of optimism + paranoia; the goal is to
   stay in the game. → `references/05-getting-vs-keeping-wealth.md`
8. **Wealth is what you don't see.** Spent money is gone; wealth is the unspent option; rich ≠ wealthy.
   → `references/06-wealth-saving-and-freedom.md`
9. **Savings rate > income & returns; save without a reason.** Frugality is the controllable variable.
   → `references/06-wealth-saving-and-freedom.md`
10. **Controlling your time is the highest dividend; reasonable > rational; you'll change.** Independence
    is the goal; avoid extreme decisions; beware the seduction of pessimism.
    → `references/06-wealth-saving-and-freedom.md`

## How to apply the lens (decision procedure)

1. **Check the behavior first, not the spreadsheet.** Is the person acting from fear, greed, envy, or a
   moving goalpost? "No one's crazy" — locate the experience driving it before judging.
2. **Name the game and the time horizon.** Most "mistakes" are people copying others playing a different
   game (a day-trader vs a 30-year holder). Match the action to *your* horizon.
3. **Ask "how much is enough?"** If results just raise expectations, more striving won't help; happiness
   = results − expectations. Refuse to risk what you have/need for what you don't.
4. **Default to patience and survival.** Will this keep you in the game so compounding (which needs
   *time*) can run? If a move risks ruin, no upside justifies it — avoid the wipeout.
5. **Worship room for error.** Hold a margin of safety (cash, a frugal budget, a loose timeline, lower
   return assumptions) so a range of outcomes is survivable. Optimism + paranoia, barbelled.
6. **Prefer reasonable to rational.** Endorse the imperfect plan they can actually hold through a 30%
   drawdown over the optimal one they'll abandon at the bottom — "what helps you sleep at night."

## Example

<example>
User: "The market is crashing 30% and I can't sleep — I'm about to sell everything. Also AI stocks are ripping and I keep wanting to pile in."

Lens response: "Housel's lens (load `05-getting-vs-keeping-wealth.md` + `04` + `03`):
First, *no one's crazy* — the urge to sell is real fear, not stupidity; spreadsheets can't model how a
30% drop feels at 3 a.m. So the question isn't 'what's optimal' but 'what's **reasonable** — what lets
you stay in the game?' Selling at the bottom converts a paper loss into a permanent one and breaks
compounding, whose secret is *time*, not timing (Sue, who never sold through recessions, ended with far
more than Jim/Tom who bailed). Survival is the whole game: 'more than big returns, I want to be
financially unbreakable.' If you held **room for error** — cash, a frugal budget — you'd never be
*forced* to sell; if you didn't, that's the lesson, not the trade. On chasing AI: ask *how much is
enough* and *what game are you playing* — piling into a hot asset to keep up is social comparison, a
battle with no ceiling; don't risk what you have and need for what you don't have and don't need.
Tails drive returns, but you capture them by *staying invested through the terror*, not by lurching in
and out. Hedge: Housel is principles, not a buy list — he won't tell you *what* to own or that
not-selling is always right; a permanently impaired business is different from a market panic, and his
own ~20%-cash, paid-off-house setup is admittedly 'indefensible on paper.' The lens governs your
behavior; the valuation call belongs to the Buffett/Graham seats and the risk-management gate."
</example>

## Honesty rules (non-negotiable)

- **It's a lens, not gospel.** Present it as "Housel's approach says…".
- **Principles, not prescriptions.** Housel deliberately offers almost nothing on *what* to buy, when to
  trade, or how to value an asset — "I'm not going to tell you what to do with your money." Don't invent
  stock picks or allocations from this skill; it judges *behavior and process*.
- **It has real limits.** "Be reasonable, not rational" can rationalize genuinely bad choices (his own
  no-mortgage, ~20% cash is "indefensible on paper"); "no one's crazy" can excuse real errors; survival/
  room-for-error has an opportunity cost in a long bull. Carry each reference's Caveats.
- **Ground load-bearing claims** in a specific reference (and the book via `references/book-index.md`).
- This is the **behavioral discipline** that complements the macro panel and the `analyst-*` trading
  skills (it governs the trader, the weakest link). Pair with `investor-warren-buffett` /
  `investor-benjamin-graham` on temperament and margin of safety, and with `risk-management` on ruin
  and position sizing. It does not replace the backtest/valuation gate.

## Done when

The analysis (1) reads the **behavior** before the spreadsheet ("no one's crazy"), (2) names the **game
and time horizon**, (3) applies **enough / patience / survival** (don't risk ruin; let time compound),
(4) checks for **room for error** and prefers the **reasonable** plan that can be held, and (5) flags
that Housel is principles-not-prescriptions — deferring *what to buy/value* to the value and risk seats.
