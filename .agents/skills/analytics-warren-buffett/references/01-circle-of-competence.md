# Circle of Competence — Warren Buffett's Framework
> Source: Berkshire Chairman's letters (1977–2024, berkshirehathaway.com/letters), esp. 1992, 1996, 1999, 2014; the Owner's Manual; "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville" (1984); annual-meeting Q&A. Distilled 2026-06-07. Letters all reachable; older HTML (1977–2003), PDF (2004–2024).

## Core thesis
Invest only inside the boundary of what you genuinely understand — your "circle of competence." The size of the circle matters far less than knowing precisely where its edge is. Buffett's edge is not a wider circle than everyone else's; it's the discipline to refuse anything outside it, no matter how exciting. Most investing errors come not from analytical mistakes within the circle but from straying outside it and mistaking a great *story* or a great *business* for a great *stock* — a wonderful company bought at a foolish price is a bad investment. "Risk comes from not knowing what you are doing." Predictability is the gatekeeper: he wants businesses whose economics he can forecast a decade out with high confidence, which is why he passed on most of technology for forty years and concentrated in consumer brands, insurance, and simple cash machines.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Circle of competence**: define the perimeter honestly; operate ruthlessly inside it. "You don't have to be an expert on every company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your circle of competence."
- **The "too hard" pile**: most opportunities go here on purpose. You are paid for the few you understand, not for activity.
- **Great business ≠ great stock**: economic quality and purchase price are two separate decisions; both must clear.
- **Predictability filter**: only forecast what is forecastable. Reject businesses whose 10-year economics you can't sketch.
- **You don't get paid for difficulty**: a 1-foot bar you can step over beats a 7-foot bar you might clear (Munger/Buffett).
- **No called strikes**: investing is a game with no umpire forcing you to swing — wait for your pitch.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- Bought **See's Candies (1972)** and **Coca-Cola (1988, ~$1B, ~7% of KO)** — consumer products he could understand and whose demand curve he could predict for decades.
- Avoided tech through the dot-com era explicitly because he could not handicap which firms would have durable economics 10 years out (1999 Sun Valley/Fortune): few got rich from autos or aviation despite their transforming the world — being right about the *industry* doesn't tell you who captures the *profit*.
- **Apple (began 2016, became ~$120–150B position, Berkshire's largest)**: he reframed it not as a tech bet but as a consumer-products company with extraordinary brand/ecosystem lock-in — bringing it inside the circle on consumer-franchise grounds, not engineering grounds. (Trimmed heavily 2023–2024.)
- **IBM (2011, ~$10–13B)**: a tech foray that disappointed and was exited — illustrates the danger near the edge of the circle.
- **Dexter Shoe (1993)**: paid in BRK stock for a business whose moat (low-cost domestic shoe manufacturing) evaporated under foreign competition — a competence/durability misjudgment he calls one of his worst.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Before any thesis, write one paragraph in plain language explaining how the business makes money and why it still will in 10 years. If you can't, mark it "too hard" and stop.
2. Score predictability explicitly (high/medium/low). Only act on high; demand a deeper margin of safety for medium; reject low.
3. Separate the two questions on every name: (a) Is this a good business? (b) Is the price sensible? Require BOTH a "yes." A great business at a silly price is a pass.
4. Treat narrative excitement ("transformative," "AI," "the future") as a yellow flag — being right on the trend rarely identifies the profit-capturer.
5. Keep a "too hard" pile and be proud of it. Inactivity on names you don't understand is correct behavior, not failure.
6. When operating near the edge (a new sector), size smaller and demand a bigger discount; treat it as provisional until proven.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- The competence boundary made him **miss the entire FAANG/early-internet wealth creation** (Google, Amazon, Microsoft) — he has called passing on Google and Amazon mistakes of omission. A narrow circle has a real opportunity cost in growth regimes.
- He **evolved** the circle for Apple — proof the boundary should be revisited, not frozen, but he reframed rather than learned the underlying technology.
- The lens favors stable, predictable cash businesses and structurally underweights high-uncertainty, high-optionality winners — a poor fit for early-stage or platform-disruption investing.
- "Understanding" can become a rationalization; Dexter and IBM show even Buffett misjudged durability inside what he thought was his circle.

## Memorable quotes

- "What counts for most people in investing is not how much they know, but rather how realistically they define what they don't know."
