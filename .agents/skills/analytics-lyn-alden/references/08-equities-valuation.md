# Equities, Valuation & Strategy — Lyn Alden's Framework

## Core thesis
Valuation determines forward returns far more than narrative does: the price you pay sets the ceiling on what you can earn. No single metric is trustworthy — every ratio breaks in some regime — so you triangulate with several, normalize for the business cycle, and demand a margin of safety because the future is genuinely unknowable. Most individual investments are mediocre for *passive outside* holders (the math is brutal: a tiny minority of stocks generate all the wealth), so the durable edge for an individual is not stock-picking genius but high savings rate, diversification, valuation-discipline, staying the course, and exploiting the one advantage pros lack — no career risk, so you can be contrarian and patient.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **CAPE / Shiller PE**: price ÷ 10-yr inflation-adjusted average earnings. Smooths cyclical earnings noise that wrecks trailing P/E. Strongly *inversely* correlated with subsequent 10–20yr returns. Use relatively (across countries/sectors/time), not as a timing trigger.
- **The cyclicality trap of P/E**: trailing P/E looks *highest* at recession bottoms (earnings collapsed → cheap is mislabeled expensive) and *lowest* at peaks (peak margins → expensive is mislabeled cheap). P/E alone systematically inverts the real signal.
- **Fair-P/E-by-growth ladder**: 0% growth → P/E ~10; 2% → 12; 4% → 15; 10% → 25; 20% → 40+. 10% earnings yield (P/E 10) is the no-growth baseline. Paying P/E 75 is fragile: a slip from 20%→15% growth drops fair value to ~50.
- **Better valuation tools** (use the right one per business): Price-to-Free-Cash-Flow (cuts through net-income accounting games; essential for REITs/infra where depreciation distorts net income); PEG and dividend-adjusted PEG (Lynch's <1 rule; add dividend yield to growth in the denominator); Market-cap-to-GDP (Buffett indicator, ~60% at recession lows to ~150% at peaks); CAPE. DCF is "the closest thing to a universal method."
- **DCF done honestly**: value = sum of future cash flows discounted to present. The math is easy; predicting the future is the hard part — "both an art and a science." Build inputs bottom-up (history of revenue/margins/share-count + management guidance), avoid false precision. Protect against error with **margin of safety + diversification**, NOT by jacking up the discount rate to hide uncertainty (that buries the real distribution of outcomes in one opaque number). Fair value is a *range*, not a point.
- **"Most investments are bad" math**: of ~26,000 US stocks 1926–2019, only 4% beat T-bills; the other 96% collectively only *matched* T-bills; 86 stocks produced half of all excess return. Globally 2.4% of firms created all net wealth. Most assets exist to benefit owner-operators/employees/customers, not passive outside investors.
- **Financial Blackjack (leverage arbitrage)**: borrow abundant fiat cheaply (below money-supply growth, ~7% US avg) and deploy into scarcer assets. Banks ~10:1, real estate ~5:1, Apple/P&G permanent corporate debt, Berkshire (insurance float + bonds). Decent operations are "platforms to attach fiat shorts to." This drove decades of returns but erodes as rates normalize.
- **Shareholder yield** = (dividends + buybacks) / market cap. Target ~8%+ for slow growers, ~5%+ for fast growers.
- **Business cycle (4 phases)**: Recovery → Normalcy → Bubble → Recession, driven by debt expansion/contraction and central-bank rates. The *stock* cycle leads the *economic* cycle — peaks come months before recessions.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- Meb Faber: cheapest-quartile countries by CAPE returned 3,052% (1993–2018) vs 962% S&P 500 — >3x.
- Active funds: ~85% lag the S&P 500 over 5yr; 92–95% over 15yr. Average *investor* earned ~2.6%/yr vs 7.2% for the index (behavior gap).
- Pros underperform partly from **career risk** — fear of deviating from benchmark prevents the contrarian bets that generate alpha.
- Long-run asset race 1928–2023: $100 → T-bills $2,249, T-bonds $7,278, gold $10,042, real estate ~$5,360. Gold matched dollar dilution (money supply 400x, gold ~100x nominal); purchasing power vs commodities ~flat.
- Buybacks: companies that return the most cash (divs+buybacks) outperform over decades. BUT bad timing is the norm — heavy buybacks at 2007 peaks, then halted in the 2009 trough (exactly backwards). HP/IBM bought back stock over R&D and lost relevance. Travelers case: shares 669M→281M (−58%) over 2007–17, EPS/book/dividend-per-share all roughly doubled, stock +385% vs S&P +287% — "literally all of Travelers' returns came from dividends and buybacks," aided by buying at ~12x P/E.
- Crashes: 2008 S&P −55% peak-to-trough in ~18 months. Recovery times: 2008 ~4.5yr; 2000 ~7yr (15yr real); 1929 ~24yr (27yr real); Nikkei still below its 1989 peak decades later. In 2008 prices fell 41% but dividends only 21% — dividend stocks cushioned. 60/40 recovered in 3yr vs 4.5yr for all-stock.
- Flagged bubbles: student-loan debt (~$100B→$1.2T+ in a decade), US healthcare (~18% of GDP, growing ~6%/yr, 2x per-capita cost with no better outcomes), unfunded state pensions (~$4T, assuming unrealistic 7–8% returns), federal debt (near WWII levels as % GDP). Individually survivable, collectively a drag on middle-class consumer spending.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
- Never value off a single ratio. Triangulate CAPE + cap/GDP + P/FCF + dividend-adjusted PEG + DCF, and pick the tool that fits the business (P/FCF for REITs/infra, PEG for growers, CAPE/cap-GDP for broad allocation).
- Treat trailing P/E with suspicion at cycle extremes; normalize earnings (10yr average, mid-cycle margins) before judging cheap/expensive.
- For any growth stock, sanity-check the implied P/E against the growth ladder and stress-test a modest growth disappointment.
- In DCF, output a *range* (bear/base/bull cash-flow paths). Use a normal discount rate; absorb uncertainty via margin of safety + diversification, not an inflated rate.
- High CAPE / high cap-GDP → tilt *gradually* (small moves) toward cheaper regions/sectors and raise quality/dividend exposure; do NOT make a binary all-in/all-out timing bet.
- Prioritize savings rate over chasing return — contributions compound more reliably than alpha. Avoid the killers: high fees, market timing, buy-high/sell-low, over-conservatism.
- Demand durable moats (network effects, brands, patents, oligopoly, scale) and tilt toward historically winning sectors (tech, telecom, energy, healthcare); be valuation-sensitive on mediocre businesses.
- On buybacks: favor consistent multi-decade repurchasers with long-tenured, owning management in boring mature sectors buying *after* R&D and *when undervalued*; avoid high-turnover CEOs chasing EPS-target bonuses. Judge by shareholder yield.
- Prepare for crashes in good times: diversify across asset classes and regions, rebalance on a schedule (forces buying weakness), keep low leverage and high savings. Only buy protective puts when vol is low AND stocks expensive AND a bubble is evident — otherwise premium decay bleeds you.
- Exploit the individual's edge: no career risk → be patiently contrarian and stay invested through recessions.

## Caveats / where she hedges
- "There are no perfect valuation metrics." Each has a failure mode: CAPE distorted by accounting/tax-rule changes, sector mix, and small/structurally-shifting indices; cap/GDP confounded by offshore earnings and private-vs-public GDP.
- DCF is only as good as its forecasts; garbage in, garbage out.
- Valuations can stay elevated for years without correcting (e.g., 1997–2000) — high CAPE predicts *long-run* returns, not timing.
- Survivorship bias inflates historical equity returns (many markets were wiped out by war/expropriation).
- The 4-decade tailwind of falling rates / fiat leverage is unlikely to repeat — don't extrapolate it; the next regime needs a different framework.
- "There's no silver bullet" — no strategy fully protects against everything; hedging usually loses money over time.
