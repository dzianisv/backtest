## Core thesis
Bitcoin is a **monetary commodity** — a scarce, bearer, non-cash-flow digital object acquired *for its own sake* as portable capital, like gold. Its value comes from a **monetary premium** sustained by network effects, not from utility/throughput. This is the key split:
- **Monetary protocols** (Bitcoin) can accrue and hold large value because money is winner-take-most and network-driven.
- **Utility protocols / tokens** (most of crypto) will *not* accrue durable value: as commoditized rails, their fees trend toward the marginal cost of operation. The *users* of the network capture the value; *holders* of the token do not.
Therefore Bitcoin is the exception, not the rule — and "crypto" as a category is mostly a different (worse) bet than Bitcoin specifically.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Monetary good vs. cash-flow asset.** Stocks/bonds throw off dividends/interest and are valued by DCF. Bitcoin and gold throw off nothing; they are valued by scarcity × adoption (monetary premium). Do NOT DCF a monetary good — and do NOT expect a utility token to behave like a monetary good.
- **Value accrues to users, not holders (the Pfeffer argument).** "You don't need to hold the coin of a network to use it; you just need to buy some and use it for fees in the moment." A useful utility blockchain can grow enormously while its token stays cheap, because competition pushes fees toward marginal operating cost.
- **Commoditized rails race to marginal cost.** Analogies: the entire ETF industry manages ~$13T but is worth <$200B combined; NYSE+Nasdaq parents (~$140B mcap) clear $60T+ in volume yet are worth less than Ethereum alone. Infrastructure that moves huge value captures thin margins.
- **Network effect / Metcalfe's Law.** Value scales ~with the square of users (one-sided ≈ 0.5·n²; two-sided ≈ a·b). Money is the ultimate network-effect good — liquidity, security, and recognition compound and self-reinforce.
- **The 10x displacement barrier.** A challenger must be "obviously superior by an order of magnitude" to dislodge an entrenched network. Bitcoin "hasn't yet been displaced in twelve years despite the arrival of over 8,000 competitors." Forking the code is trivial; forking the community/liquidity is not ("I could technically copy Wikipedia… would I be a real challenge? Of course not").
- **Protocol simplicity = Lindy longevity.** Like TCP/IP (1970s, still foundational), simple focused protocols survive decades via incremental upgrades. Bitcoin "focuses on doing one thing very well: being a decentralized and secure store of value."
- **Layered scaling.** Don't judge a settlement layer by base-layer TPS. Settlement on L1, frequent reversible payments on L2 (Lightning) — mirrors how traditional finance layers retail rails atop settlement. Value settled per unit time is effectively limitless given enough market cap + layers.
- **Pricing in BTC / BTC as hurdle.** Because Bitcoin is the hardest-supply monetary network, it functions as the benchmark the rest of crypto must beat: an alt is only worth holding vs. Bitcoin if it can credibly win and *retain* market share against Bitcoin's network effect. Most can't, so most underperform BTC over a full cycle.
- **Three explicit assumptions any honest crypto valuation must state:** (1) max units that can ever exist *across all competing coins* (not just one coin's cap), (2) future economic activity/adoption in the asset, (3) market-share retention vs. competitors.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Credibly fixed supply.** Hard cap of 21M; "Satoshi Nakamoto himself couldn't add more coins to the Bitcoin protocol if he wanted to at this point." No central party can change it — the credibility of the limit is the product.
- **Scarcity of a coin ≠ scarcity of coins.** "Although the units of any individual cryptocurrency are scarce, unlike precious metals there is no scarcity at all when it comes to the total number of all cryptocurrencies that can exist." Unlimited new tokens = dilution risk for the category.
- **Dominance.** Bitcoin ≈ 60%+ of total crypto market cap (recovered from <40% in 2017); BCH, BSV, LTC each <1–2% of BTC mcap with negligible relative hash rate. Most altcoins never reclaimed 2017 highs.
- **Halving cycle.** Issuance 50→25→12.5→6.25 per block, ~every 4 years. "Even if the annual new interest in Bitcoin from new buyers remains just constant (rather than growing), Bitcoin's price is likely to rise in value over the course of a halving cycle." Stock-to-flow rising toward / past gold's (~50–60, projected >100).
- **Security via hash rate.** Continuously rising ATH hash rate; attacking it costs "as much electricity as a small country." Competing chains have orders of magnitude less security.
- **Macro tailwind.** Era of large deficits / negative real yields / money-printing → demand for scarce assets. "Even a 1% spillover into Bitcoin from the tens of trillions' worth of zero-yielding bonds and cash assets… would be far larger than Bitcoin's entire current market capitalization."
- **Valuation methods (ranges, not point estimates):** quantity theory MV=PT; comparison to national M2/M1 money supplies; and store-of-value as % of global wealth (gold ≈ 2–3% of global wealth as the comp). All hinge on adoption assumptions — wide outcome distribution.
- **90% of gold demand is non-industrial** → monetary commodities are valued by network/monetary premium, not industrial utility.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. **Classify first: monetary good or utility token?** If the asset is "rails for something else," default to *won't accrue durable value to holders* — price it like commoditized infrastructure (margins → marginal cost), not like money.
2. **Never DCF a monetary good; never assume a utility token captures its network's growth.** Match the valuation method to the asset type.
3. **Use Bitcoin as the hurdle rate.** For any alt, ask: can it win an *order-of-magnitude*-better position AND *retain* market share vs. Bitcoin's network effect? If not 10x better, reject — "its chances are remote against the likes of bitcoin."
4. **Score the moat on network-effect inputs:** liquidity, security/hash rate, dominance %, ecosystem depth (custody, hardware, on-ramps), Lindy/age, dedicated holder base. Bitcoin scores uniquely high; rank everything else below it.
5. **Demand the three assumptions** (total units across all coins, future economic activity, retained share) before accepting any bullish crypto valuation. Reject hand-waving.
6. **Distinguish risk from fraud.** Bitcoin = risky speculation, not a Ponzi. Assess it on probability of taking/holding persistent monetary market share — that is the real bear case, not "no intrinsic value."
7. **Judge settlement layers on settled value + layers, not base-layer TPS.** "Too slow" is a category error for an L1.
8. **Size for volatility, hold through cycles.** Expect halving-cycle rhythm and local overbought phases; position-size rather than time it. Dollar-cost-average is the default accumulation method.

## Caveats / where she hedges
- Genuine outcome uncertainty: Bitcoin could become major monetary infrastructure (justifying/exceeding current valuation) OR stall and fall far — hinges on unknowable future adoption and network behavior.
- Current mcap can outrun current economic activity: "Bitcoin's market cap is in the same ballpark as Singapore's M1 money supply, even though it has far fewer active users." Valuation embeds optimistic future adoption.
- "Price is what an investor pays, but value is what an investor gets" — current price reflects expectation, not present utility.
- Regulatory risk is real but declining with scale (institutional/political ownership); was most acute below ~$1T mcap. Gold's 1933–1975 U.S. ban shows bans are hard but possible.
- She separates technological soundness (high conviction) from valuation/timing (humble). Volatility is expected during an emerging-store-of-value phase and should fall as mcap grows.
