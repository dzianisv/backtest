# The Monetary System / What Is Money — Lyn Alden's Framework

> Source: lynalden.com. Distilled 2026-06-07. Articles: "What Is Money?" (/what-is-money/), "The Four Monies" (/the-four-monies/), "Banks, QE, and Money-Printing" (/money-printing/), "The Risks of Fractional Reserve Banking" (/fractional-reserve-banking/), "The Federal Reserve Is Now Broke" (/broke-federal-reserve/), "The Importance of Price Signals" (/price-signals/), "Gold and Bitcoin" (/gold-and-bitcoin/).

## Core thesis
Money is the *most salable good* in a society — it emerges bottom-up from liquidity and salability, not from state decree (Menger). The deepest distinction is **money (an asset that is nobody's liability — a bearer asset like gold or self-custodied bitcoin) vs. currency (a liability of a bank or state — a ledger entry).** Modern fiat is pure abstraction held up by taxation and network effects; "money printing" is best understood mechanically through base vs. broad money and *who* creates each. Distorting money distorts the price signals economies rely on to coordinate.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Money = most salable good.** The asset people most reliably can trade for what they want. Salability has three axes: across **space** (portable), across **time** (durable/holds value), and across **scales** (divisible). Sound money also needs: fungibility, verifiability, scarcity.
- **Money vs. currency.** Currency is a *liability* of an institution (a dollar is your asset but someone else's IOU). Money is a liquid fungible asset that is *no one's* liability. Crystallized by Russia 2022: "Russia's gold is money; their FX reserves are currency" — $300B+ of fiat reserves were frozen; gold outside borders could not be.
- **Bearer asset vs. ledger asset.** Bearer = you control it, no counterparty risk (physical gold, self-custodied BTC). Ledger = depends on an institution honoring a promise (bank deposits, FX reserves). Bearer assets that are *also* liquid/fungible/portable are remarkably rare — that's the whole point of gold and bitcoin.
- **Stock-to-flow (S2F).** Existing supply ÷ annual new production. High S2F = hard to debase. Gold ~60-70x (annual mining ~1.5-2.0% of above-ground stock). The core vulnerability of any money is technology making *production* easier (Yap rai stones collapsed when Europeans brought better quarrying tools).
- **Money is always a ledger of consensus.** Even commodity money relies on collective agreement about who owns what (Yap's oral ledger; Bitcoin's distributed ledger). Different systems just differ in *who keeps the ledger* and how tamper-resistant it is.
- **Base money vs. broad money.** Base = currency in circulation + bank reserves at Fed + Treasury's general account. Broad = base + commercial bank deposits people actually transact with. ~4 broad dollars exist per base dollar in the US. The Fed controls base; it cannot directly control broad money distribution.
- **The Four Monies (allegory of competing monetary systems):**
  1. **Golden Monarch** = commodity money (gold) — reigned millennia, now aging.
  2. **Lord Sam / "The Uncle"** = US dollar / fiat central banking — sits "at the Cantillon Source," showing decay.
  3. **Dragon Emperor** = state-controlled digital currency (CBDC / yuan) — surveillance and control of all subjects.
  4. **Archmage Nakamoto** = Bitcoin — decentralized, permissionless, even its creator can't control it.
- **Price signals as the economy's nervous system.** Prices solve a *bandwidth problem* central planners can't: "a room full of guys dictating prices just can't keep up with the bandwidth." Price = pain: undesirable but a useful signal to redirect resources.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Three ways money/credit is created:**
  1. **Bank lending** — a loan creates a matching deposit; total reserves unchanged. Raises the money *multiplier* (broad÷base), not base money.
  2. **QE alone** — Fed creates reserves to buy existing assets (Treasuries/MBS) from banks. "QE alone is not inflationary and mostly stays in bank reserves." 2008-2014 QE (~$3.5T) recapitalized banks; most never entered broad money, so no CPI surge.
  3. **QE + fiscal deficits (MMT in practice)** — Treasury spends into the real economy and the Fed monetizes the debt. This is the *only* mechanism that directly raises broad money without bank lending. 2020: Fed +$3T in three months, direct household stimulus, vertical jump in bank deposits; M2 grew ~40% over 2020-2021 → "some degree of price inflation... was almost inevitable."
- **"The Fed can lend but can't spend. The Treasury can spend, but... needs the Fed to buy some of its sovereign debt."** Together they bypass individual constraints (2020 SPVs bought corporate bonds via Treasury backstop).
- **Fractional reserve banking:** banks fund long-duration illiquid loans with on-demand deposits = **maturity mismatch**. ~4 broad dollars per base dollar; bank leverage was ~23-to-1 right before 2008. A solvent bank can still fail from a *liquidity* run if depositors demand cash at once; when the whole system is levered, no bank can rescue another.
- **A "broke" Fed:** Assets = mostly long-duration Treasuries/MBS at low fixed rates; Liabilities = reserves + reverse repos + currency. After raising rates fast from Sept 2022, the Fed pays more on liabilities than it earns on assets → operating losses. It records a **deferred asset** (a placeholder) instead of marking negative equity, and keeps functioning because it controls its own ledger. Real consequences: Fed remittances to Treasury (~$100B/yr in good years, ~$1T cumulative 2011-2022) stop → bigger deficits; interest now flows to commercial banks instead.
- **Inflation framework:** broad money growth is "necessary but not sufficient." Inflation needs *both* persistent broad-money expansion *and* physical supply constraints. Structural deflationary forces (high private debt, aging demographics, technology, wealth concentration, outsourcing) suppress inflation between fiscal bursts.
- **Structural deflation baseline:** technology continuously lowers production cost; priced in gold, wheat/copper fell over 150+ years. "True" inflation ≈ broad money growth, not CPI.
- **Gold vs. Bitcoin:** Gold ~1 oz above-ground per person; "a 1-oz gold coin could buy a top-notch outfit a century ago, and can today." Bitcoin = first credible *digital* bearer asset / digital scarcity: 21M cap, ~1-2% new supply, halvings every 4 years, self-custody via seed phrase, fastest asset to $1T market cap; ~2-3% of people globally own it (emergent store of value). Bitcoin's risk = thousands of clones threatening the perception of supply limits; mitigated by first/biggest network effect and security.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
- When asked "is X money or currency?", check: **is it someone else's liability?** If yes → currency (has counterparty/confiscation risk). If no and it's liquid/fungible/portable → money/bearer asset.
- When assessing debasement risk of any monetary asset, compute or estimate **stock-to-flow** and ask "can technology cheaply increase its supply?" Low S2F or easy production → avoid as a store of value.
- When evaluating inflation risk, **track broad money growth (M2) and fiscal deficits, not QE headlines.** QE alone ≈ disinflationary plumbing; QE + large deficits monetized = genuine money printing. Require *both* money growth and supply constraints before forecasting sustained inflation.
- When you see price controls, shortages, or "fixed" rates, flag a **suppressed price signal** — expect misallocation, shortages, or hoarding, and distrust the reported price.
- For sovereign/counterparty-risk scenarios (sanctions, capital controls, bank fragility), prefer **bearer assets self-custodied outside the relevant jurisdiction** over ledger claims.
- When analyzing a central bank's solvency, remember **negative equity "won't matter at first"** mechanically — focus instead on lost Treasury remittances, who receives the interest, and the second-order fiscal/inflation feedback loop.
- Distinguish **liquidity crises from solvency crises** in banks; a run can kill a solvent institution via maturity mismatch.

## Caveats / where she hedges
- Inflation is *path-dependent*: structural deflationary forces (debt, demographics, tech) can dominate, so money growth alone won't guarantee CPI inflation — a one-time injection can be absorbed.
- Presents the MMT/Mosler view fairly ("the difference between money and litter is whether there's a tax man") as *why states choose* fiat, not as proof fiat is superior.
- Bitcoin is framed as an **emergent** store of value with genuinely uncertain terminal market cap; clone risk and adoption (~2-3%) are open questions, not settled.
- A "broke" Fed is mechanically survivable; the concern is long-run structural fiscal imbalance, not imminent collapse.
