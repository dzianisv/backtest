# The Dollar System & International — Lyn Alden's Framework
> Source: lynalden.com. Distilled 2026-06-07. ## Core thesis
The dollar's global role is a structurally self-undermining machine. Reserve-currency status forces the issuer to run persistent current-account deficits to supply the world with dollars (Triffin's dilemma), which hollows out its industrial base and accumulates external debt until the arrangement becomes unsustainable. Simultaneously, the world has borrowed ~$13T offshore in dollars it cannot create, producing a structural "short squeeze" that periodically rockets the dollar higher, breaks emerging-market debtors, and eventually forces the Fed to monetize deficits — the resolution that weakens the dollar over a multi-year horizon. The petrodollar/reserve system is fraying (de-dollarization, central-bank gold buying, the dollar weaponization backlash) but multipolar drift is gradual (5-10+ yr), not a sudden collapse. For investors, the practical edge is *valuation + the dollar cycle*: a weak-dollar decade favors commodities, EM, and international over an expensive, structurally-deficit U.S.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Triffin's dilemma (two flavors).** Bretton Woods version = *capital account* flaw (can't keep gold backing while supplying global dollars). Petrodollar version = *current account* flaw (must run trade deficits to export dollars, but the deficits compound into unsustainable debt + deindustrialization). "A currency can't be the world's reserve currency forever."
- **The global dollar short squeeze.** Offshore borrowers with dollar liabilities are involuntarily *short* dollars. A rising dollar raises their real debt burden, forcing them to buy more dollars to service it — a self-reinforcing squeeze. Strong dollar = de-facto global QT; weak dollar = global QE.
- **Balance-of-payments hierarchy.** Current account (trade + income flows) is the *top* of the currency-strength hierarchy, above interest rates and monetary-policy noise. NIIP (net international investment position) is the stock that the current-account flow accumulates into.
- **Three dollar bull cycles since 1971** (1980s → Plaza Accord; 1990s-2000s → EM crises; 2015-present → repo stress + Fed monetization). Each cycle does damage at *lower* dollar strength because global leverage/interconnection is higher.
- **"It's hard to weaken your currency into a trade surplus."** Competitive devaluation has a natural floor: once QE/low rates flip the trade balance to surplus, the currency stabilizes (Japan 2012-15, Switzerland). Conversely a structural deficit currency (USD) has the *weakest* foundation despite looking strong.
- **Currency-war ammo inversion.** Don't ask who looks strongest; ask "who has the most ammo to devalue from here." The U.S., having tightened while Japan/Europe exhausted QE, has the most room to debase.
- **Valuation-driven geographic allocation.** Buy the cheapest markets by CAPE/P/E/P/B, not market-cap weight. Equity outperformance rotates between regions over decades driven by *valuation change*, not fundamentals.
- **Market-cap-weighting problem.** Cap-weighted international indices overweight slow-growth, indebted, expensive economies (Japan 18-25%) and underweight EM (~12% despite most global growth).
- **EM four-layer risk pyramid** (analyze separately): (1) company fundamentals, (2) market valuation multiples, (3) exchange-rate risk, (4) capital-flow risk (foreign reallocation moves small markets disproportionately).
- **Debt-cycle counter-cyclicality.** Most people lever up when times are good and are forced to deleverage in busts. Invert: cut debt / hold liquidity at late-cycle high-valuation peaks; deploy at early-cycle low valuations.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Offshore dollar debt:** $2.3T in 2000 (23% of U.S. GDP, 50% of M2) → $12.1T Q3 2019 (56% of GDP, 78% of money supply); ~$13T+ creates structural dollar demand.
- **U.S. relative decline:** share of global GDP ~40% (1945) → 35% (1971) → 20-25% (2020). Went from largest creditor to largest debtor; NIIP ≈ −50% of GDP (~$29T assets vs ~$42T foreign-owned).
- **Fed/Treasury plumbing:** Fed now owns more Treasuries than all foreign central banks combined; foreign Treasury holdings peaked ~Aug 2019 then fell; banks had to absorb ~$3.9T new issuance 2015-19 → Sept 2019 repo spike → Fed monetization pivot. March 2020 Treasury market "ceased to function effectively."
- **De-dollarization:** Russia→China exports 98%→33% USD (2014→2020); Russia→Europe 69%→44%; Russia-India ~100%→~20%. China stopped accumulating Treasuries ~2013, redirected surpluses into Belt & Road, launched yuan oil futures.
- **U.S. debt-to-GDP:** 40% (1985) → 55% (2002) → 105%+ (2020). Current account deficit ~2.5% GDP now (peaked ~6% pre-2008).
- **Currency-war case studies:** Japan 2012-15 yen 78→120/USD (~35-63% devaluation) but stabilized on current-account reversal; Switzerland's franc resisted QE+negative rates due to surplus.
- **Currency risk on returns:** yen fell ~63% vs USD 2011-15 — gutted dollar-based Japanese returns despite sound fundamentals.
- **Global debt:** ~$250T, ~320% of world GDP (up from ~280% a decade prior). Italy gov debt 130%+; Japan gov 200%+ ("most indebted overall"); Australia household 120%+. Lowest-debt: Russia, India, Mexico, Thailand. China $3T reserves buffer corporate leverage.
- **International value edge:** cheapest-25%-of-countries (1993-2018) returned 3,052% vs S&P 962% (>3x). "Better to buy EM historically undervalued than U.S. historically overvalued."
- **EM valuations:** MSCI EM ~7.6x P/E, 1.9x P/B vs U.S. ~23x P/E, 4x P/B. MSCI EM ~10% annualized 2000→ vs ~5% MSCI World. EM ~20%+ drawdown roughly every 2 yrs; buying the −20% selloffs → median +23% (1yr) / +44% (5yr).
- **S&P 500 earns 40%+ revenue abroad** → strong dollar suppresses translated earnings (corporate profits flat 2014-19 tracking dollar strength).

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
- **Read the dollar cycle as a global liquidity regime.** Rising DXY = tightening for global debtors → de-risk EM/commodities, expect EM debt stress; falling/peaked dollar after a Fed-monetization pivot → rotate INTO EM, commodities, international, and away from expensive U.S.
- **Anchor long-term currency views on current account + NIIP, not rates.** Persistent deficit + deeply negative NIIP = structurally weak currency (USD). Surplus + positive NIIP = resilient (JPY, CHF, EUR area to a degree).
- **Treat offshore-dollar-debt stress as a squeeze signal.** Watch repo rates, foreign Treasury holdings, and Fed balance sheet. A funding squeeze precedes the policy pivot that turns the dollar down.
- **Allocate international by valuation, not cap weight.** Tilt to cheapest CAPE/P/B countries; deliberately overweight EM vs its ~12% index weight; dodge cap-weight concentration in indebted Japan/Europe. Use dividend funds (VIGI/VYMI/IDV) layered on cap-weight (VXUS), or selective single-country ETFs, to spread away from one country.
- **Run the 5-checkpoint country screen:** (1) growth (population + IMF GDP), (2) debt %GDP across gov/household/corp via BIS, (3) trade balance, (4) FX reserves %GDP (defense capacity), (5) stock valuations vs own history.
- **Use EM volatility as the entry mechanism, not a deterrent.** Systematically buy 20%+ EM selloffs; rebalance to enforce buy-low/sell-high.
- **Hedge/size for FX risk explicitly** — a great EM equity call can be erased by a 60% currency move; foreign-currency-denominated EM debt compounds the danger.
- **Position counter-cyclically on debt.** Late cycle (high valuations + high leverage): cut personal debt, hold liquidity. Early cycle (cheap): deploy.
- **Hold neutral reserve assets for the fraying.** Gold (central-bank accumulation, crisis backstop/collateral) and, speculatively, Bitcoin, hedge the multipolar de-dollarization drift.

## Caveats / where she hedges
- Structural change is *slow*: 3-5 yr base case for dollar weakness, 10+ yr for system restructuring. "Predicting a sharp change to the global monetary system just around the corner is always going to be improbable, even if one day one of those improbable things happens."
- Tail risks trigger temporary dollar *rallies / deflationary spikes* — pandemic-style shocks, a European sovereign crisis, military conflict — even within a secular weakening thesis.
- No fixed timing on the petrodollar's end; base case is gradual multipolar drift (energy priced in multiple currencies, regional/digital reserve baskets), not 1971-style overnight collapse.
- International value can underperform for a *full decade* (EM lagged U.S. through the 2010s) before mean-reverting — the thesis needs patience.
- Bitcoin treated speculatively, not as a confirmed reserve asset.
