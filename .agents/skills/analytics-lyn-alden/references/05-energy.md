# Energy as the Base Layer — Lyn Alden's Framework
> Source: lynalden.com. Distilled 2026-06-07. All five fetched, no 404s. Note: the renewables piece carries a Dec-2023 author retraction ("no longer reflects my views, the strong performance has already played out") and contains no technical framework — used only for the growth-narrative counterpoint.

## Core thesis
Energy is the master resource of the economy: everything else — food, metals, manufacturing, GDP itself — is a derivative of energy. Economic output is essentially energy converted into goods and services, so the cost and availability of energy sets the floor under all real activity. The 2020s face structural energy scarcity caused by a decade-plus of capex starvation in hydrocarbons + nuclear, compounded by policy that discourages high-EROI sources while subsidizing low-EROI ones. Until something cheaper AND more reliable than fossil fuels exists at scale, energy constraints will drive persistent background inflation. The investable implication: a multi-year oil/gas/uranium bull market, and a structural drag on energy-intensive activity (reshoring, manufacturing, EVs).

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Energy as master resource.** Strong correlation between GDP/capita and energy/capita. Growth = more energy used OR more efficient use of energy. De-growth is not neutral: "a country that pursues de-growth can expect to be geopolitically dominated by countries that don't." Higher energy throughput is what lets the planet sustain its current population (carrying capacity).
- **EROI (Energy Return On Investment).** Net energy out vs. energy spent to get it. Civilization needs high-EROI sources. High-EROI (nuclear, hydro, oil/gas) exploit *natural* concentration; low-EROI (solar, wind) require *engineered* concentration + storage. Weissbach et al. (2013): nuclear ~40–60x, oil/gas ~20–50x, gas CCGT mid-pack, wind ~5–8x and solar ~3–6x *after buffering* (i.e. once you add the storage needed to make them usable, EROI collapses). Below a threshold EROI, a source cannot power a complex society on its own.
- **"Area under the curve."** What matters is not the *peak* price or *peak* capacity — it's the *integral* over time: cumulative energy cost, or cumulative energy delivered. A $200 oil spike that crashes = "a few unusually high bills." Sustained $90+ for years forces "permanent shifts in how [households/businesses] operate." Likewise for renewables: nameplate capacity ≠ delivered energy; the area under the generation curve (capacity × capacity factor × time) is the real number, and intermittency keeps that area far below the headline.
- **Capex starvation = bull case.** Rick Rule: "bear markets are the authors of bull markets." The 2014 crash + 2020 COVID shock + ESG divestment cut global upstream capex ~60% off the 2014 ~$700B peak. 250+ oil/gas bankruptcies (2015–2020) made survivors prioritize free cash flow over growth. Underinvestment today = supply shortfall tomorrow while legacy wells deplete.
- **Energy mix is additive, not substitutive.** New sources historically *add* to the stack rather than replace prior ones in absolute terms. Solar/wind grow fast in % terms while coal/oil stay flat or grow in absolute TWh (India, Germany). Electricity is only ~20% of total energy consumption — electrification alone doesn't decarbonize the other 80%.
- **The offshoring lever is spent.** The 2000s oil shock was absorbed disinflationary by offshoring manufacturing to China. That counterweight is gone in the 2020s (and reshoring reverses it), so energy cost inflation now hits margins directly.
- **Three layers of capital (reshoring lens).** Rebuilding industry needs industrial capital (factories) + energy capital (electricity/fuel) + human capital (skilled technicians). The factory is the easy part; energy and skills are the binding constraints.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Monetary overlay:** M2 +40% since early 2020. Oil priced in *gold* sits near historic midline even when nominal prices cause stress — nominal prices step up permanently (1970s, 2000s pattern) via debasement + commodity cycle.
- **Analyst baselines too low:** Morningstar modeled Chevron on $60 oil / $3.30 gas vs. 2022 reality of $100+/$6; futures curves assume normalization Alden disputes.
- **Underinvestment evidence:** shale capex below 2018 levels despite high prices; drilled-but-uncompleted well inventory depleted; OPEC+ spare capacity thin. US shale on record FCF at low capex (discipline, not growth).
- **Intermittency signal:** negative electricity pricing now ~5–6% of hours nationally (vs 1–2% a decade ago), 20%+ in heavy wind/solar regions — proof of supply/demand timing mismatch (overproduce when unwanted, short at peak).
- **Storage limits:** batteries have far lower energy density than hydrocarbons, short lifespans, need scarce metals. Buffered renewable EROI and payback periods stretch to decades — unsuitable for fast-growing, energy-starved economies that need power *now* (fast coal payback wins there).
- **Waste/lifecycle:** ~8M metric tons of end-of-life solar panels by 2030, 80M by 2050; <10% of US panels recycled (toxic lead → landfill). Wind blades ~one per commercial-aircraft-wing, ~8,000/yr in US, "there forever" (Veolia). China makes ~70% of solar gear on coal power (Xinjiang forced-labor concerns).
- **Regional case studies:** New England blocked pipelines → imports costly LNG + burns dirtier oil. Europe 2021 gas 25→200+ EUR/MWh; "relief" came via warm weather + demand destruction (fertilizer/steel/aluminum going offline), i.e. became a commodity importer. Pakistan outbid on spot LNG — rich-world bidding starves developing nations. Germany nuclear phaseout: ~$12B/yr social cost (NBER), 70% from coal-pollution deaths; coal *substituted* for nuclear, not eliminated.
- **Demand persistence:** per-capita oil — US ~25 bbl/yr vs India ~0.2; 2.6B people lack clean cooking; petrochemicals, aviation, shipping, fertilizer, plastics all need hydrocarbons for decades. "Phasing out oil and gas would necessitate an immense change to everything."
- **Reshoring/energy gap:** China 8,833 TWh electricity vs US 4,510 TWh (US flat for ~two decades); China 12x US steel output; China dominates rare earths and 7 of top-10 container ports. TSMC Arizona delayed to 2025 for lack of skilled workers. "Rebuilding industrial capacity is like turning a giant ship."
- **Inflation mechanism:** rolling 5-yr data shows oil among the most inflationary variables. Capex-starved supply + thin OPEC+ spare capacity = low elasticity. Fed tightening / China lockdowns / strong dollar give *temporary* relief that masks the structural deficit — "if significant new energy supply doesn't come online, energy price inflation would be ready to come back whenever policymakers try to let their economies grow again."
- **Renewables counterpoint (the one she later retracted):** McKinsey — 77% of new generation capacity to 2050 from wind/solar, electricity rising 18%→25% of energy demand. Growth ≠ returns; sector is politically/technologically volatile (SunEdison bankruptcy). Stocks ran 2018–21, corrected 2022–23.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
- **Distrust normalization curves.** When a model/forecast assumes energy reverts to a low long-run price, flag it. Ask: does the *area under the curve* (years of elevated cost) break the thesis even if the spike fades?
- **Treat capex starvation as a leading bullish signal** for commodities: low/declining upstream capex + depleting legacy supply + persistent EM demand → multi-year producer bull market. Favor low-cost producers, midstream/transporters, and uranium as a diversifier (IXC, URA-type exposure; CNQ/EPD/Kazatomprom-style names she cited).
- **Separate nameplate from delivered energy.** For any renewable/grid claim, multiply by capacity factor and ask about storage/buffering before crediting it. Discount low-EROI sources for their hidden storage + lifecycle costs.
- **Model energy as an input cost to everything else.** When pricing reshoring, manufacturing, fertilizer, EVs, data centers, AI compute — add the energy-capital and energy-price constraint; assume bottlenecks (power, skilled labor) cause delays + overruns.
- **Use energy as an inflation regime indicator.** Sustained high energy + spent offshoring lever = sticky structural inflation; expect disinflation only from demand destruction (recession), not productivity. Tilt toward real/commodity-linked assets in that regime.
- **Prefer diversified/redundant energy** over single-point all-electric systems when assessing resilience risk.

## Caveats / where she hedges
- Temporary demand destruction, recession, dollar strength, or a warm winter can suppress prices for *quarters* — masking, not resolving, the structural deficit. The thesis is secular, not a short-term price call.
- Nuclear is her preferred high-EROI answer but is bottlenecked by politics and decade-long build cycles — not a near-term fix.
- She does not deny renewables grow massively (multi-decade story); her point is they're additive, lower-EROI, and growth ≠ investor returns. She explicitly retracted the bullish 2018-era renewables-investing piece in Dec 2023 (performance already played out).
- Reshoring is real and funded but slow ("turning a giant ship"); near-term it tightens commodity/energy demand rather than relieving costs.
