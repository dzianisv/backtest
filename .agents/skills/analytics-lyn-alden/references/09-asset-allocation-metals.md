## Core thesis
Diversify across **economic environments**, not just across tickers. No single asset wins in every regime (growth, inflation, deflation/recession), so hold a spread of stocks, foreign stocks, bonds, real estate, and precious metals so the portfolio compounds through all of them. Stock-picking outperformance is hard; broad balancing reliably cuts risk while preserving growth. Tilt the mix by **valuation and business-cycle position** — aggressive when assets are cheap and sentiment is fearful, defensive when expensive and euphoric. The unifying barbell: pair **scarce/monetary assets** (gold, later Bitcoin, hard real assets) that hedge currency debasement against **cashflow-producing assets** (quality equities, REITs, dividend growers) that compound. The binding constraint is behavioral: "an investment method is only good if you can actually stick with it."

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Strategic vs Tactical allocation.** Strategic = passive buy-hold-rebalance (LifeStrategy / Betterment / M1). Tactical = adjust weights, via either (a) **valuation-based** — her own method, compare CAPE, P/B, dividend yield, debt across regions/assets and rotate toward the cheap; or (b) **momentum-based** — Meb Faber 5-asset (US stocks / foreign stocks / 10yr bonds / REITs / commodities, 20% each), exit to cash below the 10-month MA, re-enter above. Momentum beats buy-and-hold on risk-adjusted basis but is tax-inefficient in taxable accounts.
- **Risk ≠ volatility.** Risk = probability of *permanent or long-lasting* loss of value. Volatility = how fast/far price moves. They correlate but are distinct: volatility is the *opportunity*; permanent capital loss is the real enemy. Economic moats (not recession-immunity) are what defend against permanent loss.
- **Rebalancing harvests volatility.** Systematic sell-high/buy-low beats static weights; her 40-yr model where she pushed to 80/20 after declines and 60/40 after surges roughly *doubled* a static 70/30's return.
- **Diversify across environments.** Bonds for deflation/recession; equities + REITs for growth; gold + commodities for inflation/currency stress; cash for optionality when bonds offer poor risk-reward.
- **Central-bank "Superman" optionality.** CBs hold overwhelming firepower they usually restrain. If they "take off the gloves" (devaluation, helicopter money, MMT-style fiscal-monetary coordination) they can override deflation — this is the asymmetry that makes long bonds dangerous and gold attractive.
- **Contrarian quality screen (bargain vs value trap):** no shorts, no/minimal margin, require an economic moat, avoid excessive debt. Low price alone is not a signal.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Sample portfolios (low-cost index ETFs, M1):**
  - *Aggressive (80% equity):* US 45% (VTI 35 + QUAL 10), Foreign 35% (VWO 15 + VEA 10 + VIGI 10), Bonds 10%, Gold 5%, REITs 5%. ~0.10–0.12% ER.
  - *Moderate (70% equity):* US 40% (VTI 30 + QUAL 10), Foreign 30% (VWO 15 + VEA 10 + VIGI 5), Bonds 20%, Gold 5%, REITs 5%.
  - *Conservative (60% equity):* US 39% (VTI 25 + QUAL 7 + NOBL 7), Foreign 21% (VWO 7 + VEA 7 + VIGI 7), Bonds 26%, Gold 7%, REITs 7%.
- Heuristics: stock allocation ≈ **100 minus age**; real-estate exposure ~25–30% baseline of net worth (adjust for primary residence). Overweight emerging markets vs market cap; add a quality factor (QUAL) and dividend growth (NOBL/VIGI).
- **Gold sizing: 5% baseline, up to 10%.** Gold is "more like a currency than a commodity" — 85–90% of flow goes to jewelry/bullion, not industry. Drivers: (1) **money-supply growth** — ~1 oz gold per person is static while currency-per-capita rises; gold should track ~5%/yr per-capita currency creation; gold-to-M2 below 2.5% matched only early-1970s and late-1990s, both pre-major bull markets. (2) **Real rates** — gold rises above-trend in negative real-rate regimes, below-trend in strongly positive ones; it is the *spread* (yield minus inflation), not inflation level, that matters (high-inflation/higher-yield 1980s hurt gold). (3) **Momentum** — S&P priced in gold; gold/S&P breaking above its 50-month MA echoed the 2001 bull launch. (4) **Under-owned** — global equities ~$90T vs all above-ground gold ~$12T; pensions/401ks hold ~none; sentiment "like late 2009, not 2011."
- **Silver:** industrial (most conductive metal — solar, electronics, EVs use ~3x ICE), so it falls with recessions, weakening its hedge role. Gold:silver ratio averaged ~15:1 over millennia, recently ~86:1 → silver relatively cheap, but more cyclical.
- **Miners vs royalty/streamers:** miners show "laughably bad performance compared to the metal" (bad capital allocation, peak-timed M&A, leverage → insolvency: "the market can stay irrational longer than miners can stay solvent"). Prefer **royalty/streaming** (Franco-Nevada, Royal Gold, Wheaton) — historically beat the metal and pay dividends.
- **Bond bubble / duration risk:** "yields have never been this low in the modern era"; >$13T negative-yielding debt premised on permanently-suppressed inflation. A 10yr bought at 2.5%, yield rising to 5.5% in a year ≈ **~20% capital loss**; longer durations magnify it. Austria 100yr @1.2% vs 1.6% inflation = negative real return *for a century*; Italy 50yr sub-3% (high-debt, no printing press). Bonds still work as recession ballast when CBs cut; junk/EM bonds behave like stocks and fail that role. Asymmetry on long sovereigns: little upside if yields compress, catastrophic downside if CBs turn aggressive. Note the uneven inflation: goods deflate but healthcare/tuition/housing run 5%+.
- **Contrarian valuation evidence:** CAPE/Shiller P/E, P/B, dividend yield, Market-Cap-to-GDP predict 5–20yr returns (not short-term). High valuation → poor forward 20yr returns and vice versa. Average investors underperform by rotating *out of* underperformers *into* what already rose — buying peaks, selling troughs.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. **Always hold a debasement hedge.** Carry 5% gold (up to ~10% when real rates are negative/falling and gold-to-M2 is depressed). Treat gold as monetary insurance, not a return engine.
2. **Cap long-duration sovereign bonds in a fiscal-dominance/inflationary regime.** Prefer short duration, cash, gold, quality dividend equities, and non-overheated real estate as the "stability" sleeve. Reserve long bonds for clear deflation/recession ballast only.
3. **Set weights by valuation + cycle, not by forecast.** Add equity risk when CAPE/Mkt-Cap-to-GDP are low and sentiment fearful; trim when expensive and euphoric. Use ~"100 minus age" as the neutral anchor.
4. **Rebalance on bands to harvest volatility** — sell winners, buy losers back to target. This, not timing, captures the structural edge.
5. **Treat drawdowns as opportunity, permanent loss as the threat.** Screen holdings for moats + low debt; never confuse a cheap value trap with a bargain (no shorts, no/low margin).
6. **Diversify internationally and tilt to the cheap** (EM/value when they have lagged); avoid concentration in high-debt low-growth markets.
7. **For metals exposure prefer royalty/streamers over miners**; use physical/ETF for the gold sleeve itself.

## Caveats / where she hedges
- Metals produce no cashflow, carry transaction/storage costs, and demand discipline against over-allocation — hence the 5–10% cap; she has timed sells (2011 @ ~$1,900) and rebuys (from 2016).
- She acknowledges genuine deflationary forces (demographics, globalization, tech, debt overhang) — the bond-bubble thesis is conditional on CBs/fiscal turning aggressive, not certain.
- Valuation predicts long-horizon returns only; it gives no short-term timing edge ("market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent").
- Tactical/momentum methods are tax-inefficient in taxable accounts.
- Contrarian edge exists *because* it is psychologically hard — "if it were easy, everyone would be doing it."
