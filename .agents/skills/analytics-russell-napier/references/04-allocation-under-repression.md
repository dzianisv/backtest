# Allocation Under Repression — Russell Napier's Framework
> Sources: The Market/NZZ interviews (2020-07-14, 2022-10-14, 2024-12); MarginalRevolution note on his allocation advice (2022-10); Behind the Balance Sheet "The Great Portfolio Reset" (substack). Distilled 2026-06-07.

## Core thesis
Under financial repression with 4-6% structural inflation and capped, negative-real rates, the central rule is: do NOT be the captive creditor. The 60/40 portfolio breaks because both legs are vulnerable — long government bonds are the instrument of confiscation (you are "robbed slowly"), and a passive cap-weighted S&P 500 is exposed to forced liquidation by repatriating foreign capital under national-capitalism rules. Own real and equity assets that benefit from the government-directed capex boom and that compound with nominal GDP: gold above all, equities geared to reshoring/defense/energy/decarbonization, value and small/mid caps, real assets, and selective non-US markets (Japan, EM, repressed-but-growing domestic champions). Keep fixed income short-duration if held at all.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Don't be the captive saver** — repression transfers wealth from creditors to debtors; structure the book as a debtor/owner, not a lender.
- **60/40 is broken** — bonds confiscated, S&P concentration + repatriation risk; the 2020-2040 mix must differ from 1980-2020.
- **Own the capex boom** — energy, defense, climate/grid, reshoring, "de-risking from China" beneficiaries; the largest investment cycle since 1994.
- **Value over growth (1966-82 analogue)** — in the last repression/inflation era, value stocks were "the only thing which protected wealth in real terms."
- **Gold as the standout** — monetary signal; reprices violently once the market accepts inflation > rates permanently.
- **Investment in solidarity / national champions** — own equities governments will protect/promote, not those they will tax or force-sell.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- Bonds: "You shouldn't own any fixed interest securities. None." Investors in government debt "are the ones who will be robbed slowly." If any fixed income, keep it SHORT-duration.
- Gold: "It's the standout asset." Bullish; expects a major repricing once people see the inflation-rate link is broken (a ByteTree reading of his 4% case implies gold ~$7,000).
- S&P 500: "You would not want to own the S&P 500" — vulnerable to forced liquidation as institutions repatriate (Macron €300bn/yr framing).
- Buy instead: "The unloved, under-owned assets: mid and small caps, as well as value stocks." Plus equities "that won't be liquidated."
- Sectors: "energy, climate change, defence, inequality, our dependence on production from China — will all be solved by massive investment"; own the companies geared to that.
- Geography: favors equities in repressed-but-growing economies and domestic-champion plays; constructive on Japan and selective EM; cautious on China currency (expects RMB to fall).
- Real assets/equities and real estate cited as inflation-protecting; cash/short rates as a parking place given negative real returns elsewhere in fixed income.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Cut long-duration government bonds to ~zero; if you hold fixed income, keep it short-duration and expect negative real returns.
2. Hold a structural gold allocation as the primary repression/monetary hedge; treat gold strength as confirmation the thesis is playing out.
3. Tilt equities to value, small/mid caps, and capex-boom sectors (defense, energy, grid/electrification, reshoring); underweight expensive mega-cap concentration.
4. Reduce reliance on a single cap-weighted S&P exposure; diversify into markets less exposed to forced repatriation — Japan, selective EM, domestic champions.
5. Prefer "national-champion" equities governments will protect/direct capital toward; avoid sectors likely to be taxed, price-capped, or force-sold.
6. Abandon static 60/40; rebuild around real assets + value equities + gold + short cash, sized to survive negative real rates.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- His allocation is downstream of the inflation-regime call — which he made EARLY (was deflationist to 2019). If disinflation back to 2-3% holds, long bonds and the S&P would beat his book (as they largely did 2023-24).
- "Avoid all the S&P" is a strong call against the asset that has dominated returns; survivorship/timing risk is high.
- He concedes near-term DEFLATION risk; in a deflation scare, gold and value can sell off first — his allocation is a multi-year regime bet, not a tactical one.
- Mainstream pushback: 60/40 recovered in 2023-24; bonds-are-dead calls have a poor track record; gold timing is notoriously hard.
