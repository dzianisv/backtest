# Anatomy of the Bear — Russell Napier's Framework
> Sources: Napier, "Anatomy of the Bear: Lessons from Wall Street's Four Great Bottoms" (Harriman House; 1st ed. 2005, 4th ed. 2016); ValueWalk summary (valuewalk.com); Citywire "12 Steps to Recovery" (citywire.com); RealInvestmentAdvice annotated PDF. Distilled 2026-06-07. Book itself is paywalled (buy); summaries free.

## Core thesis
Generational equity bottoms are NOT made at "fair value" — they are made when stocks trade at a massive discount to the replacement cost of their assets, and they coincide with the DEATH of a deflation scare. Studying the only four great US bear-market bottoms of the 20th century (Aug 1921, Jul 1932, Jun 1949, Aug 1982) via ~70,000 contemporaneous Wall Street Journal articles, Napier finds each bottom occurred at an extreme cheapness (Tobin's Q ~0.3 = ~70% discount to replacement value; Shiller CAPE in the mid-single to low-double digits) AND at the exact moment fear flipped from deflation back toward inflation. The lesson: value alone is insufficient; you wait for the macro inflection — confirmed by commodities (copper), corporate bonds, and inflation expectations turning — before the equity bottom.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Tobin's Q ratio** — market value vs. replacement cost of assets; great bottoms occur near Q ≈ 0.3 (only times equities were >70% below replacement value).
- **Cyclically-adjusted PE (CAPE / Shiller PE10)** — bottoms clustered at CAPE roughly 4.7-11.7; cheapness is a necessary precondition.
- **"Deflation kills equity"** — every great bottom coincides with the death of a deflation scare; the inflation/deflation tipping point is THE decisive signal.
- **Recovery sequence** — government bonds turn first (too early to use), then corporate (BAA) bonds, then commodities/copper stabilize and rise, then equities bottom a few months later.
- **Society-shift backdrop** — each bottom births a new era: 1921 consumer society, 1932 big government, 1949 military-industrial complex, 1982 rebirth of free markets.
- **Economy & market bottom together** — at extremes the 6-9 month "stocks lead economy" rule breaks; they trough nearly simultaneously.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- Four bottoms: Aug 1921, Jul 1932, Jun 1949, Aug 1982 — all in summer.
- Valuation at bottoms: corporate America on ~30% of replacement value (Q ≈ 0.3); Shiller PE between 4.7 and 11.7.
- Copper is the most reliable commodity bellwether — it stabilizes/rises before the equity bottom (in 2009 example, ~$3,200 → ~$3,900/tonne).
- BAA corporate bonds bottom (yields peak) before equities — "BAA bonds always bottom out before equities." (2008-09: BAA yield peaked 9.54% on Oct 31, fell to 8.45% by Mar 19.)
- Inflation expectations turning up (TIPS breakevens recovering from near-zero) marks the death of the deflation scare.
- Research base: ~70,000 WSJ articles in the two months either side of each of the four bottoms.
- Note: government-bond prices turn "much too early" to be a usable timing signal; use corporate bonds + copper instead.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. For a generational buy signal, require BOTH conditions: extreme valuation (CAPE in single/low-double digits, Q near 0.3) AND a dying deflation scare.
2. Do not buy on cheapness alone — wait for the macro inflection to confirm; cheap can get cheaper through the deflation phase.
3. Read the bottoming sequence in order: corporate (BAA) bond yields peaking, then copper/commodities stabilizing and rising, then inflation expectations (TIPS breakevens) recovering — THEN buy equities.
4. Ignore the "stocks lead economy by 6-9 months" rule at extremes; expect near-simultaneous troughs.
5. Use CAPE and Q to gauge how far a market is from a generational bottom — today's high CAPE means the US is far from one, regardless of drawdowns.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- The 2005 book's forward call was DEFLATIONARY and BEARISH: he argued China-driven deflation would eventually drag the S&P to a 1982-style bottom (he floated an S&P ~400 / "fall 70%" target). That generational US bottom never came on his timeline — a major early/wrong call, and the root of his decade as a deflationist.
- Only four data points; the sample is tiny and US-specific; pattern-matching to other markets/eras is fragile.
- Q and CAPE are long-horizon valuation tools, useless for timing within years; he is explicit that bottoms also need the macro trigger.
