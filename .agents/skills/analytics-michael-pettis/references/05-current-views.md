# Current Views (2023-2026) — Michael Pettis's Framework
> Source: Carnegie "What's New about Involution?" (2025-08-26, reachable); Carnegie "Is China's High-Quality Investment Output Economically Viable?" (2026-04, reachable); Carnegie "What GDP Means in a Soft Budget Economy Like China" (2026-04, reachable); Carnegie "Foreign Capital Inflows Don't Lower U.S. Interest Rates" (2025-07-07, reachable); Foreign Affairs "How Tariffs Can Help America" (2024-12, 402 paywall); IMF F&D "Behind the Veil of Tariff Fixation" (2025-09, 403); X threads Feb/Apr 2025 (403). Distilled 2026-06-07. Every claim dated below.

## Core thesis
As of mid-2026, Pettis's read is that China's slowdown is structural, not cyclical, and is now being managed by pivoting from a popped property bubble into a manufacturing/high-tech-capex bubble that exports overcapacity and deflation worldwide ("Second China Shock"). He sees Trump's 2025 tariffs as economically defensible *in principle* (the US over-consumes and under-produces) but largely wasted in practice because they are bilateral and erratic rather than systemic. On de-dollarization he is a skeptic: no rival is willing or able to absorb the world's excess savings, so the dollar's burdensome role persists. The global system still requires rebalancing — surplus economies must raise household consumption — and remains stuck because rebalancing is politically costly everywhere.

## Key frameworks / mental models (current emphasis)
- **Involution = structural overcapacity** (2025): the new official buzzword for an old problem; expect it to fade like prior slogans without a real consumption shift.
- **Soft-budget GDP** (2026): China's GDP increasingly measures activity, not value, because the financial system funds loss-making output; trust the consumption share and debt path, not headline GDP.
- **Second China Shock** (2025): property bust → state-directed manufacturing capex → export of deflation to US/EU.
- **Tariffs help only systemically** (2024-2025): right diagnosis, wrong (bilateral) instrument under Trump.
- **Dollar burden persists** (2025): inflows don't cut US rates; de-dollarization is slow because no willing absorber exists.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data (dated)
- **2023:** China's consumption contributed ~82.5% of GDP growth — its best year, yet still below the ~85-90% needed for a decade to rebalance (cited Feb 2025 on X).
- **2024-12 (Foreign Affairs):** tariffs *can* help the US now (unlike the 1930s) because "Americans consume far too large a share of what they produce"; goal is to counter the "pro-consumption and antiproduction orientation," not protect champions.
- **2025-04 (X):** Trump's new tariff round shows little "systemic thinking"; because trade resolves systemically not bilaterally, it is "unlikely to be very helpful."
- **2025-07-07 (Carnegie):** "net foreign capital inflows do not lower American interest rates (unless they do so by raising U.S. unemployment)"; inflows can make domestic productive investment *less* desirable.
- **2025-08-26 (Carnegie):** "involution" is a concentrated form of structural excess capacity; deflation from unsold inventory, price wars, wage cuts (solar firms shed ~1/3 of workforce); China invests >40% of GDP vs ~25% elsewhere; raising consumption share 10pts over a decade at 4% GDP growth needs consumption to grow 5.9%/yr; "it is the rising debt burden that sets the limit."
- **2026-04 (Carnegie):** China's "high-quality" / high-tech investment can coexist with systematic overinvestment — visible tech progress does not refute the overcapacity thesis; in a soft-budget economy, reported GDP overstates real wealth creation.
- **De-dollarization (2025):** persistent skepticism — the burden of absorbing global savings has no taker, so USD's role endures even as its costs to the US real economy continue.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Treat China data through the soft-budget filter: weight the consumption share and total debt growth over reported GDP (2026 view).
2. Expect continued Chinese export pressure and global goods-deflation while the manufacturing-capex bubble runs; position trade-exposed industrials in the US/EU accordingly.
3. Read 2025-2026 US tariffs as inflation-with-limited-reshoring unless they become broad and systemic and savings actually shift.
4. Stay short the de-dollarization-is-imminent narrative; demand a *willing absorber* before pricing USD reserve loss.
5. Track Chinese rebalancing only via a rising household income/consumption share; treat slogans ("involution," "dual circulation") as non-events.
6. Watch China's debt-funding capacity as the timing variable — the model grinds, it does not snap on schedule.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- His China-overinvestment/debt call is long-running (since ~2011-2013); the slow grind validates the *direction* but his framework is hard to time and arguably hard to falsify (the limit is "whenever debt funding stops").
- 2026 critics still argue he understates genuine Chinese productivity and tech upgrading (the "high-quality investment" debate is live).
- His tariff stance is frequently misread as pro-Trump; he is conditional and critical of the bilateral execution — a nuance lost in both camps.
- De-dollarization skepticism could be wrong if geopolitics (sanctions, reserve diversification) forces a structural break his pure-economics framing under-weights.
