# Tariffs & Who Actually Pays — Michael Pettis's Framework
> Source: Foreign Affairs "How Tariffs Can Help America" (https://www.foreignaffairs.com/united-states/how-tariffs-can-help-america, 402 paywall but exists, Dec 2024); IMF F&D "Behind the Veil of Tariff Fixation" (https://www.imf.org/en/publications/fandd/issues/2025/09/point-of-view-behind-the-veil-of-tariff-fixation-michael-pettis, 403 at fetch, Sep 2025); worldtradelaw.net interview (https://ielp.worldtradelaw.net/2024/12/discussing-tariffs-with-michael-pettis/, reachable, Dec 2024); Asia Times "Pettis Paradigm" (reachable); X thread 1907629516043784509 (403). Distilled 2026-06-07.

## Core thesis
Tariffs are widely misunderstood from both sides. Free-trade economists say tariffs are simply a tax paid by domestic consumers; trade hawks say they punish foreign exporters and force reshoring. Pettis says both miss the systemic point. A tariff is best understood as a **tax on consumption that is simultaneously a subsidy to domestic production**. Whether it helps depends entirely on the macro context: in an economy like the US that **over-consumes, under-saves, and under-produces** (because it absorbs the world's excess savings), a properly designed broad tariff can redirect demand toward domestic output, raising production, employment, wages, and savings while cutting debt. But **bilateral** tariffs aimed at a single country are nearly useless, because trade and capital flows are systemic — capital and goods reroute through third countries, and the deficit is set by the capital account, not by any one trading relationship. Who actually "pays" is therefore the wrong question; the right question is how the tariff shifts the savings-investment balance.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Tariff = consumption tax + production subsidy.** The incidence is split and indirect, not a simple sticker-price tax.
- **Systemic, not bilateral.** Trade can only be rebalanced systemically; bilateral balances are "pretty useless" and bilateral tariffs leak through third countries.
- **Context-dependence rule.** Tariffs help only where the economy suffers from excess consumption / deficient production; in a balanced or savings-short economy they would hurt (as in the 1930s, when the US already over-produced).
- **Currency offset / dollar overvaluation.** Because the dollar is structurally overvalued (he cites ~20-30%) by reserve-currency capital inflows, exports are taxed and imports subsidized; tariffs partly counteract this invisible tax. Weaker import demand can also push the currency down, offsetting part of the tariff.
- **Who bears it depends on demand elasticity and FX.** If exporters cut prices to keep market share, they bear it; if not, domestic buyers do — but the *macro* outcome (production vs. consumption) is what matters.
- **Surplus country can be hurt more.** A country that depends on running surpluses to absorb its own excess capacity has fewer ways to adjust; a broad tariff regime can hurt the export-dependent surplus economy more than the deficit economy that gains production.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- Dec 2024 (Foreign Affairs): "Today, [unlike in the 1930s], Americans consume far too large a share of what they produce, and so they must import the difference from abroad" — which is *why* tariffs could help now though they hurt in the 1930s.
- Success criterion: a tariff "is successful... if it raises domestic production by enough to pull consumption up with it." The aim is "not to protect specific manufacturing sectors or national champions but to counter the United States' pro-consumption and antiproduction orientation."
- Mechanism (Pettis paradigm): tariffs "redirect a portion of US demand toward increasing the total amount of goods and services produced at home," yielding "higher employment, higher wages, and less debt."
- Dollar overvaluation of ~20-30% acts "like an invisible tax on exports and a subsidy for imports."
- On Trump's 2025 round (Apr 2025 X): "It is hard to see much systemic thinking in the new round of tariffs, and because trade can only be resolved on a systemic basis, and not on a bilateral basis, this means that they are unlikely to be very helpful."
- Bilateral balances "may impress those who don't understand trade and capital flows, but are pretty useless."

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Before judging any tariff, classify the imposing economy: over-consuming/under-producing (tariff *can* help) vs. balanced/savings-short (tariff hurts).
2. Discount bilateral, country-specific tariffs heavily — assume leakage through third countries and little net rebalancing.
3. Score a tariff by whether it plausibly raises *domestic production faster than it cuts consumption*, not by who pays the customs bill.
4. Net the currency response: expect partial FX offset, and treat broad tariffs as a counterweight to reserve-currency dollar overvaluation.
5. Identify the most export-dependent surplus partner as the party with least room to adjust — it can absorb more of the damage than headlines suggest.
6. Pair any pro-tariff thesis with a check on retaliation, supply-chain rerouting, and whether savings/investment actually shifts — without that, expect inflation with no reshoring.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- Many trade economists (Simon Lester/worldtradelaw, PIIE, Dmitry Grozoubinski, Cafe Hayek) argue empirically most of the 2018-2025 US tariff cost fell on US consumers/importers, with little reshoring — contradicting the "production subsidy" optimism.
- His own case is conditional: he does **not** claim tariffs are generally good; he says they help *only* under specific imbalanced conditions, and Trump's bilateral, erratic version largely fails that test.
- Critics note he undervalues efficiency/welfare losses and retaliation, and that "redirecting demand to home production" assumes idle capacity and slack that may not exist.
- He concedes tariffs are a blunt second-best; capital-flow controls (a Tobin-style tax) would address the root cause more directly.
