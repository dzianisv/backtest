# The Dollar as Exorbitant Burden — Michael Pettis's Framework
> Source: Carnegie "An Exorbitant Burden" (https://carnegieendowment.org/posts/2011/09/an-exorbitant-burden, reachable, 2011-09); Foreign Policy version (402 paywall); Carnegie "Foreign Capital Inflows Don't Lower U.S. Interest Rates" (https://carnegieendowment.org/china-financial-markets/2025/07/foreign-capital-inflows-dont-lower-us-interest-rates, reachable, 2025-07-07); *Trade Wars Are Class Wars* (2020). Distilled 2026-06-07.

## Core thesis
Reserve-currency status is an **"exorbitant burden," not an exorbitant privilege**. Because the US runs the world's deepest, most open, most liquid capital markets and imposes no capital controls, it is the residual absorber of everyone else's excess savings: when surplus countries (China, Germany, oil exporters) suppress domestic consumption and export savings, those savings flow into US assets by default. This forced inflow strengthens the dollar (overvaluing it by ~20-30%), which taxes US exporters and subsidizes imports, hollowing out tradable manufacturing — **deindustrialization**. To avoid the unemployment that capital-driven deindustrialization would otherwise cause, the US must raise domestic demand through rising debt (household, then fiscal) — or accept higher unemployment. So the trade deficit, low US savings, rising debt, and financialization are all the *same phenomenon*: the cost of absorbing the world's savings glut. The "privilege" of cheap borrowing is illusory, because the extra Treasuries the US must issue to fund the gap rise one-for-one with foreign demand, neutralizing any rate benefit.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Exorbitant burden (inversion of Giscard d'Estaing's 1965 "exorbitant privilege").** Reserve status is a net cost to the US real economy.
- **Excess-savings absorber.** Reserve-currency status *is* the role of absorbing the world's surplus savings; it is unavoidable given open capital markets.
- **The trilemma of absorption.** Absorbing foreign savings must produce one of: more household debt, larger fiscal deficits, or higher unemployment. There is no costless channel.
- **Triffin-style dilemma, generalized.** To supply the world's reserve asset and absorb its savings, the US must run persistent external deficits, eroding its own production base.
- **Capital-account openness as the transmission mechanism.** It is the *openness* of US capital markets (not deliberate policy) that makes the US the default destination — surplus countries "push" capital, they are not "pulled" by US demand.
- **The interest-rate myth.** Foreign bond buying does not lower US rates net of the extra issuance it forces; if anything it works through unemployment.
- **Financialization / rent-shift.** Dollar primacy tilts the US economy toward rents (finance, asset inflation) and away from productive labor and capital — falling labor + capital share of income.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- Dollar overvaluation of roughly **20-30%** attributable to reserve-currency capital inflows — "an invisible tax on exports and a subsidy for imports."
- 2011: "U.S. manufacturers are thus penalized by the overvalued dollar and so must reduce production." And: "If a rise in foreign savings is passed on to the United States... U.S. savings must decline."
- 2025-07-07 (Carnegie): "net foreign capital inflows do not lower American interest rates (unless they do so by raising U.S. unemployment)." Inflows "can actually make [domestic] investment less desirable" by strengthening the dollar and cheapening imports.
- The accounting identity that net inflows equal investment-minus-saving does **not** mean inflows raise investment; the gap can close via falling saving (debt, deficits, joblessness).
- Asymmetry: "China fully controls its banking system... and even stricter capital controls — unlike the United States," so surplus countries actively push savings into the open US market.
- Policy implication: the US could limit counterproductive inflows with "a kind of Tobin tax" on capital inflows — addressing the burden at the capital account rather than via tariffs alone.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Treat the US trade deficit, low savings, rising debt, and financialization as one linked symptom of absorbing global savings — not four separate problems.
2. Don't credit reserve status with "cheap funding"; assume the rate benefit is offset by the extra issuance the absorption forces.
3. When global excess savings rise (China surplus widens, EM/oil savings climb), expect upward pressure on the dollar, US asset prices, debt, and the deficit.
4. To reduce the deficit durably, look for measures that cut the *capital inflow* (capital controls / Tobin tax) — not just tariffs or fiscal austerity.
5. Read a stronger dollar driven by inflows as bad for US tradable production, even when it looks like a vote of confidence.
6. For "will USD lose reserve status?" — judge by whether another economy is *willing and able to run persistent deficits and absorb the world's savings*, not by gold, BRICS rhetoric, or settlement plumbing.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- Mainstream view holds reserve status *does* confer real benefits (lower borrowing costs, seigniorage, geopolitical leverage); Pettis's "pure burden" framing is a minority position critics call overstated.
- His identity-based reasoning shows the channels but, critics argue, doesn't prove the dollar is *net* harmful once seigniorage, crisis-time safe-haven funding, and flexibility are counted.
- "What replaces USD" is underdetermined in his framework: he argues no current candidate (euro, RMB with capital controls, BRICS) will accept the absorber role, so de-dollarization is slower than headlines claim — a hedge that also limits the actionable upshot.
- Overvaluation estimates (~20-30%) are approximate and contested.
