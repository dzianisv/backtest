# Analytics: The Michael Pettis Lens

Apply Michael Pettis's framework to a question. This skill is the **synthesis + router**; the detail
lives in `references/`. He is the panel's **trade / capital-flows / China seat** — orthogonal coverage
the others don't provide. Load the relevant reference before a load-bearing claim.

## The unifying worldview (everything connects to this)

Pettis reasons from **accounting identities and capital flows**. The trade balance is *identically*
savings minus investment (`S − I = current account`), so trade is a **residual**, not a driver — and
for an open economy with deep markets, the **capital account drives the current account** (capital
flows in first; the trade deficit is the consequence). Imbalances are not about thrift or
competitiveness; they come from **income distribution**: when a country suppresses the household/wage
share (via repressed wages, weak safety nets, an undervalued currency, negative real deposit rates), it
suppresses consumption, forces up savings, and must export the resulting demand deficiency as a surplus
— "trade wars are class wars." That surplus **must land somewhere**: the deficit country (especially the
US, which runs open capital markets and the reserve currency) absorbs it via rising household debt,
bigger fiscal deficits, or higher unemployment. China is the archetype of the producer-subsidy model;
the US dollar's reserve role is an **exorbitant burden**, not a privilege.

## Core mental models (the load-bearing ones)

1. **Savings identity `S − I = CA`.** The trade balance is identically savings minus investment; trade
   is a residual. → `references/01-trade-imbalances-are-capital-flows.md`
2. **Capital account drives current account.** Capital flows in first; the trade deficit follows — not
   the other way round. → `references/01-trade-imbalances-are-capital-flows.md`
3. **Trade wars are class wars.** Imbalances come from domestic inequality suppressing consumption, not
   from thrift or cheating. → `references/01-trade-imbalances-are-capital-flows.md`
4. **The adjustment must land somewhere.** Absorbing foreign savings forces rising debt, bigger fiscal
   deficits, or unemployment — no costless channel. → `references/01-trade-imbalances-are-capital-flows.md`
5. **Producer-subsidy / financial-repression model.** Undervalued FX + suppressed wages + negative real
   rates transfer income from households to producers (China). → `references/02-china-growth-model-rebalancing.md`
6. **Investment past the point of viability.** Beyond a threshold, investment destroys value while
   reported GDP keeps rising (soft-budget GDP). → `references/02-china-growth-model-rebalancing.md`
7. **Involution = structural overcapacity** → price wars and exported deflation.
   → `references/02-china-growth-model-rebalancing.md`
8. **Tariffs = consumption tax + production subsidy; only *systemic* tariffs work, not bilateral.**
   → `references/03-tariffs-and-who-pays.md`
9. **The dollar is an exorbitant burden, not privilege** — reserve status forces the US to absorb the
   world's excess savings → overvalued dollar, deindustrialization, debt. → `references/04-dollar-exorbitant-burden.md`
10. **The interest-rate myth + no willing absorber.** Foreign inflows don't lower US rates (except via
    unemployment); de-dollarization is slow because no rival will run the deficits to absorb global
    savings. → `references/04-dollar-exorbitant-burden.md`

## How to apply the lens (decision procedure)

1. **Write the identity first.** For any trade/imbalance question, start from `S − I = CA` and ask which
   term is actually moving and *why* (distribution, not morality).
2. **Trace the capital flow.** Who is exporting savings, who is absorbing them, and through which
   channel (debt, deficit, or unemployment) does the absorbing economy adjust?
3. **For China, ask if rebalancing is happening** — is the household consumption share actually rising,
   or is the model just shifting the imbalance (property → manufacturing capex)?
4. **For tariffs, ask systemic vs bilateral.** Bilateral tariffs just reroute; only economy-wide
   measures that raise the domestic savings/consumption mix change the balance — and only help an
   over-consuming economy.
5. **Be a de-dollarization skeptic by default** — demand the mechanism: who absorbs the world's surplus
   savings instead, and why would they accept the burden?
6. **Separate framework from dated view.** The identities are evergreen; his China/tariff reads decay —
   for "what does he think now", read `05-current-views.md` and re-check his latest Carnegie post / X.

## Example

<example>
User: "If the US slaps big tariffs on China, won't that fix the trade deficit and bring back factories?"

Lens response: "Through Pettis's lens (load `03-tariffs-and-who-pays.md` + `01-trade-imbalances-are-capital-flows.md`):
start from `S − I = CA`. The US deficit is identically the US under-saving / over-consuming and
absorbing the world's excess savings via open capital markets — so unless a policy changes the
*savings-investment mix*, the deficit just reroutes to other countries, it doesn't disappear. Bilateral
tariffs on one country are the weakest form: production shifts to Vietnam/Mexico, the aggregate balance
barely moves. A *systemic* measure (e.g., a tax on capital inflows, or anything that lifts the US
savings rate) could work — and tariffs *can* help an economy that genuinely over-consumes and
under-produces, which the US does. But the mechanism is 'reduce absorbed foreign savings', not 'punish
China.' Hedge: Pettis's read that China must rebalance has been directionally right but early on timing
— the model has bent without breaking for over a decade because the financial system kept funding the
losses."
</example>

## Honesty rules (non-negotiable)

- **It's a lens, not gospel.** Present it as "Pettis's framework says…". Note that some economists
  dispute the savings-glut framing and the strength of the capital-account-drives-trade claim.
- **His China timing has been early.** The rebalancing/"model breaks" call has been directionally
  sound but slow — the binding constraint is debt capacity, not a calendar date. Carry the caveats.
- **Current positioning decays.** Date every current-view claim; re-check his latest Carnegie post / X.
- **Ground load-bearing claims** in a specific reference/source (via `references/article-index.md`).
- This skill **complements** the panel (it covers trade/China/FX the others skip) and informs
  `regime-detection`; it does not replace the `fundamental-analysis` gate.

## Done when

The analysis (1) starts from the `S−I=CA` identity, (2) traces who exports vs absorbs the savings and
through which channel, (3) reframes the question as distribution/capital-flows rather than morality or
competitiveness, (4) gives the systemic-vs-bilateral (or rebalancing) verdict, and (5) flags any
current/tactical claim as time-stamped.
