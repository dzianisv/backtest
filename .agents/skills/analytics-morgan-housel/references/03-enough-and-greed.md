# Enough & Greed — Getting the Goalpost to Stop Moving
> Source: Housel, *The Psychology of Money* (2020, Harriman House), ch. 3 ("Never Enough"). Distilled 2026-06.

## Core thesis
**The hardest financial skill is getting the goalpost to stop moving.** Modern capitalism is "a pro at two things: generating wealth and generating envy," and if expectations rise as fast as results, more striving buys no extra satisfaction — "one step forward pushes the goalpost two steps ahead," and the only way to feel caught up is to take ever-greater risk. The antidote is a sense of **"enough"**: not conservatism or leaving money on the table, but the recognition that "an insatiable appetite for more will push you to the point of regret." The deepest version of the lesson: **there is no reason to risk what you have and need for what you don't have and don't need.** People who already had everything — wealth, prestige, freedom — have destroyed it all reaching for more.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Happiness = results − expectations.** "Life isn't any fun without a sense of enough." You can raise happiness by lowering expectations as surely as by raising results — and the former is more in your control.
- **Social comparison is an unwinnable game.** The ceiling is so high no one ever reaches it (a rookie compares to Mike Trout, who compares to top hedge-fund managers, who compare to Buffett, who compares to Bezos). "The only way to win is to not fight to begin with."
- **"Enough" is not too little.** It's the realization that an insatiable appetite ends in regret. The only way to know how much food you can eat is to eat until you're sick — most people won't, because vomiting hurts more than the meal is good; the same logic should apply to money but rarely does.
- **Some things are never worth risking, no matter the gain.** Reputation, freedom, family, being loved, happiness are *invaluable* — protect them by knowing when to stop taking risk.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Vonnegut & Heller (told by John Bogle):** at a billionaire hedge-funder's party, Heller notes he has something the host never will — "enough." "Enough… is so smart, and so powerful."
- **Rajat Gupta:** from the slums of Kolkata to CEO of McKinsey, ~$100m net worth (a 5% return ≈ $600/hour, 24/7). Wanting to be in the "billionaire circle," he leaked Buffett's pending $5bn Goldman investment to Raj Rajaratnam *16 seconds* after learning it; SEC pegged the insider tips at $17m in profits. He went to prison — career and reputation destroyed.
- **Bernie Madoff:** ran a *legitimate*, hugely profitable market-making firm (~$25–50m/yr) and was already legitimately wealthy — then ran the largest Ponzi scheme in history. Both Gupta and Madoff "already had everything… and they threw it all away because they wanted more. They had no sense of enough."
- **Long-Term Capital Management (1998):** traders worth tens/hundreds of millions each took so much risk chasing more that they lost everything in the middle of the greatest bull market in history. Buffett: "To make money they didn't have and didn't need, they risked what they did have and did need. And that's foolish… plain foolish."
- **Las Vegas dealer:** asked how to win, replied "The only way to win in a Las Vegas casino is to exit as soon as you enter" — same with keeping up with others' wealth.
- **Gupta's post-prison "lesson"** ("don't get too attached to your reputation") — Housel calls "the worst possible takeaway"; reputation is invaluable.

## How to APPLY (decision rules)
1. **Define "enough" explicitly and in advance.** Decide the number/lifestyle that covers what you need and a lot of what you want — then protect the goalpost from drifting upward with each win.
2. **Manage expectations, not just results.** Before celebrating a gain, check whether your expectations just rose with it; if so, you've banked nothing in happiness.
3. **Refuse ruinous bets categorically.** Apply the LTCM test: never risk what you *have and need* (capital, reputation, freedom) for what you *don't have and don't need*. Some downsides are off-limits at any odds.
4. **Quit the social-comparison game.** Treat comparison as a battle with no ceiling; the move is to *not play* — accept you may have enough even if it's less than those around you.
5. **Watch for the moving-goalpost tell.** If you feel you're "falling behind" despite real progress, that is the goalpost moving, not reality — and the fix is mindset, not more risk.

## Caveats
- "Enough" can be misread as anti-ambition; Housel is explicit it is *not* too little — wanting to surpass peers can be healthy fuel; the danger is only when ambition outruns satisfaction.
- Where "enough" sits is personal and depends on what genuinely covers your needs/wants — it is not a universal number.
- These are extreme (often criminal) examples; the everyday versions are quieter — lifestyle creep, burnout, an allocation you can't hold — but the mechanism is identical.
