# Asset Allocation, Rebalancing & Bonds — Graham's Framework
> Source: *The Intelligent Investor* ch. 4 ("General Portfolio Policy: The Defensive Investor" — the 25–75% rule and 50/50 default), ch. 8 (rebalancing / formula investing as the practical use of fluctuation), ch. 2 ("The Investor and Inflation"), and ch. 5–6 (bond/stock components). Distilled 2026-06-07. All reachable.

## Core thesis
Allocation is **policy, not prophecy**. Graham's defensive investor splits capital between high-grade **bonds** and **stocks** and keeps each within a **25–75% band**, with a **50/50** default. You tilt toward the cheaper asset class only mildly and mechanically — toward stocks after bad markets (cheap), toward bonds after great ones (rich) — and you *never* go to either extreme, because going all-in or all-out is a market-timing bet the defensive investor has no edge to make. The decisive practical move is **mechanical rebalancing**: a fixed rule (a schedule, or a band) that forces you to sell what has risen and buy what has fallen, converting Mr. Market's volatility from a threat into a source of return and — more importantly — removing the emotional decision at exactly the moment emotion is most destructive. Bonds are held for stability and dry powder, with the constant awareness that **inflation erodes their real value**, which is precisely why the defensive investor always keeps a meaningful equity component.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **The 25–75% rule:** stocks never below 25% nor above 75% (and bonds the inverse); the band itself is the discipline.
- **50/50 default + mild contrarian tilt:** start balanced; lean toward stocks when markets are depressed/cheap, toward bonds when euphoric/rich — in modest steps, never extremes.
- **Formula investing / mechanical rebalancing:** pre-commit to a rule (e.g. rebalance annually, or whenever weights drift past a band) so the buy-low/sell-high happens automatically.
- **Dollar-cost averaging:** for the accumulator, invest fixed sums on a schedule — the simplest formula plan, immune to timing error.
- **Bonds = stability + optionality, with inflation risk:** high-grade bonds dampen drawdowns and fund rebalancing buys, but their fixed nominal payments lose real value to inflation — hence never 100% bonds.
- **Allocation expresses humility, not a forecast:** you hold both because you can't reliably time the switch.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **Explicit bounds:** "We have suggested as a fundamental guiding rule that the investor should never have less than 25% or more than 75% of his funds in common stocks, with a consequent inverse range of 75% to 25% in bonds" (*Intelligent Investor* ch. 4).
- **50/50 as the neutral:** the standard division is half-and-half, adjusted only when valuations are clearly extreme.
- **Rebalancing as the mechanism that "makes fluctuation pay":** ch. 8's whole point is that the defensive investor's edge over the speculator is using volatility through a rule, not predicting it.
- **Inflation evidence (ch. 2):** Graham documents that bonds' fixed dollars steadily lost purchasing power, arguing equities and a balanced split are the defense — a point that proved understated in the 1970s inflation he warned about.

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Set a target stock/bond split inside 25–75% (default 50/50); write it down as policy *before* looking at the market.
2. Only adjust the target for *clear* valuation extremes, and only mildly (e.g. toward 60/40 when stocks are broadly cheap, 40/60 when manifestly expensive) — never 100/0 or 0/100.
3. Rebalance by a fixed rule: on a schedule (e.g. annually) or a band (e.g. ±5–10% drift), selling the winner to buy the laggard — execute the rule even when it feels wrong (it will).
4. For ongoing contributions, default to dollar-cost averaging into the target weights.
5. Hold high-grade bonds for stability and as rebalancing ammunition, but keep ≥25% equities so inflation can't quietly impoverish the portfolio.
6. Treat the allocation as the answer to "I can't time the market," not as a hidden market call.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- The **25–75 / 50/50** numbers are Graham's *general* guide for a US defensive investor; the right band depends on horizon, risk tolerance, and the bond regime — the principle (bounded, rebalanced, never-extreme) travels better than the exact percentages.
- **"High-grade bonds" assumed a positive-real-yield world** that didn't hold in the 2010s ZIRP era; when bonds yield below inflation the stability comes at a guaranteed real loss — a tension Graham's inflation chapter anticipates but doesn't fully resolve (cash / TIPS / short duration become the practical substitutes).
- Mechanical rebalancing can **rebalance into a structurally broken asset** (e.g. adding to a secular bond bear); the rule assumes mean-reverting, sound assets, not melting ones.
- Frequent rebalancing in taxable accounts incurs **taxes and costs** — band-based, low-frequency rebalancing usually dominates calendar-frequent rebalancing after frictions.
