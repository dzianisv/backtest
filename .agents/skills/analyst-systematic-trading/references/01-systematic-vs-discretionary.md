# Systematic vs Discretionary — and the Three System Types

> Source: Carver, *Systematic Trading* (Harriman House, 2015), Part One — esp. ch. 1 ("The Flawed Human Brain") and ch. 2 ("Systematic Trading Rules" / trader types). Distilled 2026-06-07.

## Core thesis
The human brain is wired to lose money. Cognitive biases — prospect theory's "get-evenitis" (we hold losers hoping to break even and snatch small gains), the narrative fallacy (we invent stories to justify trades), and overconfidence — make most people **run their losses and cut their profits**, the exact opposite of what works. A *system* is a set of objective rules that **mitigates your own biases and exploits other people's**. The point is not that computers are smarter — *you* know more than the computer; the point is that a rule is a **commitment mechanism**, Odysseus lashing himself to the mast, a line in the sand that stops you "meddling" when fear and greed strike. One modular framework serves three kinds of trader, who differ only in how much they automate the *forecasting*.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **System = commitment device, not oracle.** "Creating a purely objective system is a powerful commitment mechanism." It removes in-the-moment discretion, not knowledge.
- **The three trader types (one framework):**
  - **(a) Staunch systems trader** — fully systematic: rules generate the forecasts *and* the risk/sizing.
  - **(b) Semi-automatic trader** — *discretionary* forecasts (your opinion, turned into a number) wrapped inside the systematic risk/position-sizing framework, with systematic stops.
  - **(c) Asset-allocating investor** — does *no* forecasting at all: a constant "no-rule" forecast on a static, diversified portfolio (essentially permanent average-strength long).
- **The three design pitfalls** (the whole book is an argument against them): **over-fitting** (curve-fitting to the past), **over-trading** (paying away returns in costs), **over-betting** (too much leverage / vol target).
- **Cut losses, run profits** — the behaviour the system enforces against your instincts.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- An **"early loss taker"** (cuts losses, runs profits) beat an **"early profit taker"** in **27 of 31** futures markets studied — direct evidence the system's job is to enforce the unintuitive behaviour.
- Some FX brokers offer **500× leverage**, which Carver calls **"clinically insane"** — a setup for over-betting and ruin.
- **Annualisation convention:** multiply daily figures by **16** (≈ √256 trading days) to annualise volatility/returns-risk; equities run **≈20% annualised σ**.
- True-skill discretionary investors (e.g. Buffett) **cannot be fully systematized** — for them, be a *semi-automatic* trader (discretion in the forecast, system in the risk). HFT is explicitly **out of scope**.

## How to APPLY (decision rules)
1. **Decide your trader type first** — it determines which modules are rule-driven vs. fixed, and caps your sensible volatility target (allocator ≤20%, semi-auto ≤25%).
2. **Make the rules a true commitment** — write them down, make any deviation obvious; the value is in *not meddling*.
3. **Audit every choice against the three pitfalls:** is this over-fit, over-traded, or over-bet?
4. **Have a genuine edge or don't forecast** — if you can't articulate why a rule should work, be an asset-allocator (constant forecast) rather than inventing rules.
5. **Enforce cut-losses/run-profits structurally** (continuous forecasts + stops), because your instincts will do the opposite.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- Systematic is **not automatically better** — a true-skill discretionary investor exists; the framework's gift to them is *risk control*, not forecasting.
- The framework is for **medium-to-low-frequency** trading; **HFT is out of scope**.
- Rules mitigate *known* biases; they don't make you immune to building the *wrong* (over-fit) system — hence Parts Two/Three.
