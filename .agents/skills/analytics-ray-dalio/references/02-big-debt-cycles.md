# The Big Debt Cycle & How Countries Go Broke — Ray Dalio's Framework
> Source: Dalio, *Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises* (2018, free PDF at economicprinciples.org); *How Countries Go Broke: The Big Cycle* (Avid Reader Press, June 2025; landing page economicprinciples.org/how-countries-go-broke; full PDF economicprinciples.org/downloads/How-Countries-Go-Broke.pdf — resolves but serves compressed PDF); "An In-Depth Look at Deleveragings" (2012). Current-claim corroboration: Fortune, 14 Mar 2026 (fortune.com/2026/03/14/ray-dalio-big-cycle-debt-crisis-political-disorder-world-order). Distilled 2026-06-07.

## Core thesis
Over a long arc (~75 years, give or take ~30), short-term debt cycles ratchet debt and debt-service burdens higher until the system reaches a point where there are **more financial claims (promises to pay) than there is real output to satisfy them**. At that point a central bank faces an unavoidable choice: print money and devalue the currency, or don't print and allow a deflationary debt-default depression. **Historically the central bank always prints and devalues.** The big debt cycle therefore reliably ends in either deflationary default or currency debasement, and Dalio argues the US is now in the late, dangerous stage of one — running deficits far above what is sustainable, with debt becoming harder to finance.

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Big debt cycle** = the accumulation of many short-term debt cycles into one long supercycle (~75±30 yrs).
- **Two crisis types**: (1) **deflationary** debt crises (debt is in your own currency, default/austerity dominate, falling prices), and (2) **inflationary** debt crises (debt is foreign-currency or capital flees, devaluation/printing dominate, rising prices).
- **The four levers of deleveraging**: (1) austerity (cut spending), (2) debt reduction (defaults/restructurings), (3) wealth redistribution (haves→have-nots, e.g. taxes), (4) debt monetization (print money). Levers 1–2 are deflationary/depressing; lever 4 is inflationary/stimulative.
- **Beautiful vs. ugly deleveraging**: a *beautiful* deleveraging balances all four so nominal growth > nominal interest rates, debt/income falls, growth stays positive, inflation stays contained. *Ugly* = unbalanced → either deflationary depression or inflationary spiral.
- **Stages of the big debt cycle** (archetype): healthy/early debt growth → bubble (extrapolation, over-borrowing) → top (CB tightens or debt can't be serviced) → depression/bust (rates hit 0, monetary policy ineffective, CB prints & buys assets) → deleveraging → normalization. *How Countries Go Broke* frames the public-debt bust in stages; Dalio places the US in the **late/4th stage** of a public-debt bust.
- **Debt death spiral**: government must borrow to pay interest → more debt → higher interest → still more borrowing.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- *Principles for Navigating Big Debt Crises* analyzes **48 historical debt-crisis cases** where real GDP fell ≥ **3%**.
- **The 3% solution** (Davos 2025): US should cut the federal deficit to **~3% of GDP**; it currently runs ~**6–7%** ("we're spending ~40% more than we're taking in"). Without it, Dalio warns of an "economic heart attack."
- Big cycle length ≈ **75 years ± ~30**; last Stage-6/great-disorder period ran **1929–1945**.
- Endgame logic: "the biggest risk is that debt assets like US Treasuries will either be defaulted on or devalued — more likely devalued."
- "When a country has to print money to service its debt, that's the late stage" — monetization both finances the deficit and debases the currency, pushing investors toward gold/real assets.
- Beautiful-deleveraging condition: money-printing offsets the contraction in credit but does **not** exceed it, so it reflates without runaway inflation (Dalio cites the US 1933–37 and post-2009 as relatively beautiful; Weimar Germany 1920s as ugly/inflationary).

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
1. Identify which crisis type you're in: **own-currency debt → deflationary** (favor long bonds, cash early, then watch for monetization); **foreign-currency debt or capital flight → inflationary** (favor gold, hard assets, foreign currencies, avoid local-currency bonds).
2. When a government must print to pay interest, treat the currency and long-dated nominal bonds as **debasement risk**, not safe havens — rotate toward gold and inflation-linked / real assets.
3. Score the deleveraging on the **beautiful↔ugly** axis: is nominal growth > nominal rates with contained inflation (beautiful, risk-on OK) or unbalanced (ugly, defensive)?
4. Track the **3% deficit benchmark** and the debt-service/revenue ratio as the key US late-cycle gauges; rising debt-service-to-revenue is the death-spiral tell.
5. Don't expect precise timing — use the framework to size **tail-risk hedges** (gold, optionality) rather than to call the exact year of crisis.
6. Watch the markers Dalio names: government selling more debt than buyers want, central bank stepping in as buyer-of-last-resort, rising real yields demanded on long bonds, currency weakness vs. gold.

## Caveats / where he hedges
- **Timing**: Dalio has warned of an imminent US debt crisis for years; it has not yet broken — he concedes the cycle can run far longer than expected and says "nothing is predestined."
- Critics (e.g. MMT proponents, many economists) argue a country borrowing in its own floating currency cannot "go broke" the way Dalio frames it, and that his deterministic-cycle reading overfits history.
- The "central bank always prints" claim is a strong historical generalization; political constraints can force austerity/default instead (Greece, Argentina episodes).
- *How Countries Go Broke* (2025) is recent; its US-specific stage-placement is Dalio's judgment call, not consensus.
