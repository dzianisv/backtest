# Wyckoff Accumulation and Distribution (Composite Operator, VSA, Phase Structure)

Wyckoff analysis provides a **price-volume structural framework** for identifying when smart money — the "Composite Operator" — is accumulating or distributing a large position. It is the most interpretive and discretionary of the smart-money signals in this family, and it **confirms** phase structure after the fact rather than predicting it in advance. Use it as corroborating context for disclosed-flow and market-implied signals, never as a primary trigger.

---

## The Composite Operator mental model

Richard D. Wyckoff introduced the **Composite Operator** (CO) as a conceptual device: imagine all large informed participants — institutions, market makers, large funds — acting as a single coordinated entity with superior information, deep pockets, and the patience to accumulate or distribute without tipping their hand. The CO's imperative is:

- **Accumulation:** Build a large long position at low prices without driving prices up prematurely. Use range-bound price action, occasional shakeouts (springs) to flush weak hands, and absorb all available supply before the markup begins.
- **Distribution:** Unload a large long position at high prices without crashing the market. Use range-bound price action at a top, occasional upthrusts to trigger retail buying, and distribute into strength before the markdown begins.

The CO is a mental model, not an observable entity. It is analytically useful because it organizes a set of price-volume behaviors that do appear to repeat across markets and timeframes.

## Accumulation phases (Schematics A through E)

Wyckoff's accumulation schematic describes how a downtrend bottoms and a new uptrend begins. The canonical phase labels:

| Phase | Event | Description |
|---|---|---|
| A | Preliminary Support (PS), Selling Climax (SC), Automatic Rally (AR), Secondary Test (ST) | Selling exhaustion — PS absorbs heavy supply; SC = spike volume panic low; AR = counter-rally defining the top of the range; ST = re-test of SC lows on lower volume confirming absorption |
| B | Building a Cause | CO absorbs remaining supply in the trading range; volume often decreasing on declines (no supply) and increasing on rallies (demand present) |
| C | Spring (or Last Point of Support, LPS) | The defining event of accumulation — a brief move below the trading range lows that triggers sell-stops and flushes weak hands; reversal back into the range on increasing volume confirms the spring; **this is the highest-conviction Wyckoff buy signal** |
| D | Signs of Strength (SOS), Last Point of Supply (LPSY) | Price breaks above the range on expanding volume (SOS); pulls back to the top of the range on low volume (LPSY = last chance to add before markup) |
| E | Markup | Sustained uptrend — CO has finished accumulating; price leaves the trading range with strength |

---

## Distribution phases (mirror of accumulation)

Distribution is the mirror structure at a top — CO unloads into retail buying:

| Phase | Event | Description |
|---|---|---|
| A | Preliminary Supply (PSY), Buying Climax (BC), Automatic Reaction (AR), Secondary Test (ST) | Buying exhaustion at a top; BC = spike volume high; AR = counter-selloff defining range bottom; ST = re-test of BC highs on lower volume |
| B | Building a Cause | CO distributes supply in the range; volume increasing on rallies into supply (CO selling), decreasing on declines (no demand absorbed) |
| C | Upthrust After Distribution (UTAD) | Mirror of the spring — a brief move above the trading range highs that triggers buy-stops and attracts retail buyers; reversal back into the range on increasing volume confirms the distribution |
| D | Signs of Weakness (SOW) | Price drops below the range on expanding volume, confirming distribution |
| E | Markdown | Sustained downtrend |

## Volume Spread Analysis (VSA) — key signals

**VSA** (Tom Williams, *Master the Markets*, 2005) extends Wyckoff's price-volume logic into specific candle-by-volume patterns:

- **"No Supply" bar.** Narrow spread candle on very low volume on a down day — no selling pressure present; bullish in the context of an accumulation base.
- **"No Demand" bar.** Narrow spread candle on very low volume on an up day — no buying interest; bearish in the context of a distribution top.
- **"Effort vs. Result."** Wide spread, high volume, but small net price movement = absorption. Wide spread, high volume, large net movement = conviction in the direction.
- **Stopping Volume.** Very high volume on a down bar that closes near the high of its range — supply being absorbed by large buyers; bullish signal at lows.

## How to integrate Wyckoff into the smart-money synthesis

Wyckoff analysis belongs in the synthesis as **structural confirmation only**, not as a primary signal. The rules:

1. **Wyckoff confirms after the fact.** A spring can only be confirmed as a spring after price reverses back above the shakeout low on volume. Calling it in advance is speculation. Do not pre-announce a spring or upthrust.

2. **Use Wyckoff to validate disclosed-flow signals.** If Form 4 cluster buys are appearing during what appears to be a Wyckoff Phase B–C accumulation range, the structural context strengthens the disclosed-flow read. If insiders are buying into a trend that has no base structure, the Wyckoff context is absent.

3. **Wyckoff is discretionary and interpretive.** Two analysts looking at the same chart will identify different phase boundaries and event labels. This is a known weakness. Wyckoff contributes color; it does not change the reliability tier of the synthesis.

4. **Reliability tier.** Wyckoff/VSA sits in the middle of the reliability ranking — below real-money disclosed flows and COT extremes, but above single-print options flow. It should neither anchor nor veto a verdict from higher-reliability signals.
