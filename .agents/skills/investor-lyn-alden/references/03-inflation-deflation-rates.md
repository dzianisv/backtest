## Core thesis
Inflation is fundamentally a *monetary* phenomenon — sustained growth in the broad money supply per capita — and is only loosely connected to consumer prices (CPI), which are also moved by supply, technology, and demographics. The dominant source of money creation determines which policy works: when inflation is driven by **bank lending** (1970s), rate hikes throttle it; when driven by **fiscal deficits monetized by the central bank** (1940s, 2020s), rate hikes are impotent or counterproductive because higher rates enlarge the interest bill and thus the deficit. The 2020s are an *inflationary* debt-deleveraging (debt sits at the sovereign level, where regimes print rather than default), the structural inverse of the 1930s *deflationary* deleveraging (debt at the household/bank level, no FDIC, money supply collapsed ~30%).

## Key frameworks / mental models
- **Three inflations.** Monetary (broad money / M2 growth), consumer price (CPI on a basket), and asset price (financial assets bid up by concentrated wealth). Debating which is "real" inflation wastes time; track all three separately.
- **Money supply > velocity.** Per-capita broad-money growth is "one of the most closely-correlated variables to consumer price inflation" across 150 years (US/UK/Japan/Australia). Velocity is a weak/contrary predictor (high & falling velocity with low & rising inflation in the late 1800s; rising velocity with falling inflation in the 1990s).
- **Two engines of money creation.** (1) Banks creating deposits via private lending (money multiplier); (2) fiscal deficits + central-bank bond monetization (base + broad money). Rate hikes bite engine 1, not engine 2.
- **QE is an asset swap, not Main Street money — but fiscal deficits ARE new money.** QE moves a Treasury to the Fed's balance sheet and credits bank reserves; reserves are inert. The inflationary act is the *fiscal* spending (stimulus checks, entitlements, deficits) that QE *finances* by monetizing the debt that funds it. "The government was able to spend money on the domestic economy via the fiscal spending side that it never extracted from any existing base of money."
- **Four financing models.** Domestic borrowing (crowds out, no new money) → international borrowing (worsens NIIP) → QE (creates digital money to buy the debt) → MMT (drop the borrowing pretense; spend first, tax later, inflation is the only limit). The US has slid down this ladder.
- **Fiscal dominance / "Kobayashi Maru."** When sovereign debt >100% GDP + structural deficit >~7% GDP + supply constraints coincide, there is no clean rate setting: hike → wider public deficit & private squeeze (stagflation); cut → currency speculation & import-price inflation.
- **Transitory in rate-of-change ≠ transitory in level.** Inflation can decelerate while the price *level* never returns (1940s precedent). The new money stays in the system permanently.
- **Inflationary vs deflationary deleveraging.** Where the debt sits decides the outcome. Household/bank debt + gold standard + no deposit insurance = deflation (1930s). Sovereign debt + fiat + FDIC = the cavalry prints fast = inflation (2020s).
- **Rate sensitivity = cash-flow duration.** The further out an equity's expected cash flows, the more its valuation moves with discount rates.

## Specific claims, mechanisms & data
- **CPI understatement:** official ~2.5%/yr since 1990; her apples-to-apples estimate "closer to 3% or more." The leveraged "American Dream basket" (house, education, healthcare, car, vacation) outran CPI; quality adjustments suppress reported new-car inflation (CPI claims +22% 1990–2020 while sticker prices roughly tracked ~2.5%/yr).
- **Why 2008–2019 QE didn't inflate consumer prices:** $3.6T balance-sheet expansion was small vs ~$11T household wealth destroyed in 2008 and ~$71T (2007) net worth; ~10 deflationary offsets — technology, offshoring, onshored immigrant labor, capital-subsidized unprofitable firms, cheap commodities, wealth concentration (low-velocity billionaires; top-10% share ~60%→70%), 2008 asset crash, debt defaults, aging demographics, trade surpluses. Velocity fell to ~1.4x.
- **Repo crisis (Sept 2019):** the US "literally ran out of cash dollar lenders" for ~$1T/yr net new Treasuries (foreigners stopped, banks at ~21% Treasuries / ~7% cash regulatory bedrock); repo spiked to ~10%; Fed stepped in as buyer of last resort.
- **Rates vs inflation is weak.** 1940s inflation fell from double digits with rates held *well below* CPI — because war spending (the fiscal engine) ended, not because of rate policy. Refutes the Druckenmiller "rates must exceed CPI" claim using WWII data. Turkey/Argentina show rate policy alone doesn't tame fiscal/supply inflation.
- **Asymmetric debt rollover (2023):** private sector refinancing higher = disinflationary drag; ~half of ~$32T federal debt rolling at higher rates = inflationary stimulus (toward ~$1.3–1.9T annual interest), the two forces producing persistent stagflationary volatility.
- **Yield curve:** Fed sets only the overnight rate; it "trickles up indirectly." Inversion = investors lock in long rates expecting cuts, pushing long yields below short. Inversion kills bank net-interest margin → lending contracts → it is "both a predictor of an economic slowdown and then helps cause one… a self-fulfilling prophecy." Every US recession in 50 yrs followed an inversion (imperfect abroad: Australia 4 inversions/1 recession; Japan no inversion since 1991 despite recessions). Common gauge: 10y–2y crossing zero.
- **DCF rate sensitivity (4% discount-rate cut, 12%→8%):** value (+5% perpetual growth) +44.7%; growth (declining 20%→3%) +61.8%; hypergrowth (delayed profits) +89.9%. Equity risk premium = CAPE earnings yield − 10y yield; 2000 peak was ~ −4% (6.5% yields vs 2.5% earnings yield).
- **1930s data:** broad money fell ~30% ($47B→$32B, 1929–33); >⅓ of banks failed (no FDIC); debt/GDP ~300% by 1933. 1933 gold seizure + revaluation 1/20→1/35 oz (~40% debasement). Dollar: 1/20 oz at Dow's creation → <1/1600 oz now.
- **Creditor vs debtor:** Japan entered decline at NIIP ~+60% GDP (buffer). US NIIP went from ~−10% GDP (2008) to worse than −50% (~−$11T). "Creditor nations can more easily face deflation; debtor nations more often face inflation." Above ~100% debt/GDP, "they either default or print. Usually, they print."

## How to APPLY (decision rules for an agent using this lens)
- **Diagnose the inflation's engine before predicting the cure.** Fiscal-driven (high sovereign debt + structural deficit) ⇒ assume rate hikes fail or worsen it; expect financial repression (negative real rates, forced debt holdings), capital controls, credit restrictions, and recurring inflation waves rather than clean disinflation. Lending-driven + low public debt ⇒ rate hikes can work.
- **Forecast inflation from per-capita broad-money growth, not velocity.** Ask whether new money is reaching Main Street (stimulus/entitlements = yes; QE buying corporate bonds/MBS = no) and whether the economy is near capacity. Demand "show your work" from any hyperinflation-or-deflation-forever claim.
- **Separate nominal from real returns** in every projection for this decade.
- **Stagflation allocation hierarchy:** industrial commodities and energy first (multi-year supply constraints) → value equities (lower duration, hard-asset/inventory value, less rate-sensitive) → real estate financed with *fixed-rate* debt (liability erodes) → growth/hypergrowth equities only with caution (most rate-sensitive) → avoid cash and nominal bonds (yields below inflation). Monetary metals (gold; she also cites bitcoin) correlate with inflation eventually and shine specifically when growth stalls but inflation persists. Note nominal gains can mask real losses (Turkey stocks +2.5x local / −50% in USD).
- **Treat inflation as non-linear:** plan for disinflationary pullbacks inside an inflationary decade; don't get shaken out on the dips.
- **Treat curve inversion as both signal and mechanism:** flat/inverted + historically low-but-rising rates ⇒ position more conservatively; expect a lending/credit slowdown to follow.
- **2020s base case:** higher inflation than the 2010s; debt resolved via printing not default; tilt toward gold/silver, high-ROIC strong-balance-sheet companies, and quality foreign equities. A Dow/gold ratio dropping below ~10x is plausible before a new real high.

## Caveats / where she hedges
- Equity outcomes have "huge variance" — driven by *starting valuation*: cheaply-bought stocks crushed bonds into WWII inflation; richly-bought stocks lagged into the 1970s.
- CPI accuracy: truth sits "between the extremes" of inflation-truthers and academic defenders; some baskets over- and some under-counted.
- Hyperinflation is "very hard to occur" under current developed-world monetary structures — requires severe real-capacity loss, not just money growth; could change with legal changes to central-bank mandates.
- Monetary metals "have an eventual correlation with inflation but not necessarily at the exact moment."
- Yield-curve recession signal is "imperfect" outside the US.
- MMT's discipline (raise taxes / cut spending to fight inflation) is "unrealistic" politically — the key risk that turns managed inflation into a disorderly reset.
